# I. Public Law, Rights Enforcement, and Administrative Governance

## **Executive Orientation**

This report serves as the public-power, administrative-governance, and rights-enforcement layer of *The Legal Mastery Canon*, continuing *Volume 2: Core Operating Domains of Legal Practice*. Where the foundational volumes established the ontology of legal reality (Report A), the vertical and horizontal hierarchies of source authority (Report B), the methodology of interpreting text and constructing meaning (Report C), and the epistemic mechanics of translating reality into legally recognized proof (Report D) 1, this volume transitions the canon from private ordering to the arena of public authority and state coercion.

Public law is not courthouse bookkeeping or a collection of constitutional slogans; it is the structural framework that governs how sovereign power is constituted, allocated, constrained, exercised, supervised, and remedied. For constitutional lawyers, administrative litigators, government counsel, regulatory compliance officers, and legal AI architects, this document establishes a defense against the single most catastrophic failure mode in modern legal work: the conceptual collapse of public-law rhetoric, constitutional slogans, agency guidance, statutory authority, rights claims, judicial review, and political legitimacy into an undifferentiated grey procedural soup. By treating public law as the discipline of lawful coercion, this report provides a disciplined method for analyzing the mechanics of state power.

## **Core Thesis**

The foundational premise governing this report is absolute: **public law is the discipline of lawful coercion.** It governs how public power is constituted, allocated, constrained, exercised, supervised, and remedied. Private actors generally enjoy broad freedom to act unless a valid rule of positive law prohibits their conduct.1 In stark contrast, public actors possess no inherent freedom of action; they must trace their authority to an affirmative, lawful source and exercise it strictly through lawful procedures. Public law therefore sits at the intersection of authority, rights, institutional design, administrative process, enforcement discretion, judicial review, remedies, and systemic legitimacy.

The ultimate strength of a public-law position is determined by the operator's capacity to maintain control over the options available to the public actor and the private target. Every regulatory directive, enforcement action, administrative adjudication, and constitutional challenge must be executed not as a political argument in legal costume, but as a structured legal evaluation. The discipline of public law requires demonstrating that every exercise of state power can either successfully survive or be systematically dismantled by the application of statutory and constitutional limits.

## **The Governing Question**

Every public-law inquiry, regulatory challenge, enforcement defense, and strategic system design must answer a single, relentlessly applied programmatic inquiry:

**What public authority is being exercised, by which institution, under what source of power, against whose rights or interests, through what procedure, with what discretion, subject to what constitutional or statutory limits, and with what remedy or review available?**

To resolve this multi-variable equation under administrative and constitutional pressure, the legal operator must maintain a continuous, prospective cadence throughout the public-law lifecycle:

**Who is acting, from what source of authority, through what institutional process, affecting what right or interest, exercising what discretion, constrained by what rule, reviewed by whom, under what standard, and remedied how?**

## **Public Power Architecture Model**

To systematically analyze how public authority becomes lawful coercive action—or is exposed as an unlawful abuse of state power—the operator must utilize the fourteen-layer Public Power Architecture Model. This model functions as an interactive pipeline where decisions at any single layer immediately alter the constraints and opportunities at all other layers:

1. **Source of Power Layer**: Identify the constitution, statute, regulation, ordinance, delegation, executive authority, agency mandate, emergency power, treaty, local charter, or other legal source authorizing action.1  
2. **Institutional Actor Layer**: Identify whether the actor is a legislature, executive, agency, court, local government, public official, public corporation, government contractor, delegated private actor, or hybrid public-private institution.1  
3. **Jurisdiction and Competence Layer**: Determine whether the institution has authority over the territory, person, subject matter, status, program, benefit, license, enforcement target, or dispute.3  
4. **Action Type Layer**: Classify the public action: legislation, rulemaking, adjudication, licensing, permitting, enforcement, investigation, inspection, benefit administration, prosecution, detention, policing, spending, taxation, procurement, guidance, emergency order, or informal pressure.  
5. **Procedure Layer**: Identify the required process: notice, hearing, record, reasons, participation, comment, evidence, neutrality, disclosure, appeal, exhaustion, or emergency exception.3  
6. **Right or Interest Layer**: Identify the affected life, liberty, property, equality, speech, religion, privacy, voting, bodily integrity, family, immigration, employment, benefit, license, contract, data, or other legally protected interest.1  
7. **Discretion Layer**: Identify where the public actor has judgment, policy choice, enforcement discretion, technical expertise, prosecutorial discretion, remedial discretion, or no discretion at all.1  
8. **Constraint Layer**: Apply constitutional limits, statutory limits, procedural limits, equality limits, rights protections, federalism constraints, separation-of-powers constraints, reason-giving duties, evidentiary requirements, and non-arbitrariness principles.1  
9. **Record and Explanation Layer**: Identify what record supports the action, what reasons were given, what facts were found, what evidence was considered, and what assumptions or policy judgments were made.1  
10. **Reviewability Layer**: Determine whether review is available, blocked, deferred, exhausted, premature, moot, committed to agency discretion, barred by immunity, or limited by statute.3  
11. **Standard of Review Layer**: Identify the standard: de novo, arbitrary and capricious, substantial evidence, abuse of discretion, clear error, rational basis, intermediate scrutiny, strict scrutiny, proportionality, reasonableness, or other jurisdiction-specific standard.3  
12. **Remedy Layer**: Identify available relief: injunction, declaration, vacatur, remand, mandamus, habeas, damages, fees, reinstatement, benefit restoration, license restoration, suppression, expungement, structural relief, consent decree, or administrative reconsideration.3  
13. **Accountability Layer**: Identify democratic, judicial, administrative, professional, electoral, transparency, inspector-general, legislative-oversight, media, or public accountability mechanisms.1  
14. **Legitimacy Layer**: Evaluate whether the action is lawful, reasoned, participatory, rights-compliant, non-arbitrary, transparent, reviewable, proportionate, and socially legitimate.1

### **Distinction Rules and Common Confusions**

To maintain conceptual rigor and prevent analytical collapse across the canon, the legal operator must apply these five strict distinction rules:

* **Rule 1: Competence is Not Jurisdiction** (administrative doctrine | well-established). Jurisdiction is the fundamental power of a public tribunal (such as an Article III court) to hear and decide a case.3 Competence is the specific scope of authority delegated to an organ of the state (such as an environmental agency) to draft rules or issue permits.1 An action taken by an official outside their competence is *ultra vires* and void.1  
* **Rule 2: Absolute Removal is Not Executive Discretion** (constitutional rule | contested). While a President possesses broad Article II authority to direct executive branch officers, this does not authorize at-will removal of officers serving in independent, multi-member commissions protected by for-cause statutory tenures.8 Confusing these concepts leads to the erroneous belief that independent agencies can be dismantled by executive fiat without legislative consent.27  
* **Rule 3: Universal Vacatur is Not a Universal Injunction** (remedial doctrine | recently changed). Following *Trump v. CASA* (2025), a federal court lacks equitable authority under the Judiciary Act of 1789 to issue a universal injunction enjoining executive policies as to non-parties.20 However, under Section 706 of the APA, a court retains statutory authority to "set aside" and vacate an arbitrary regulation universally, nullifying the rule itself rather than merely enjoining its enforcement as to the litigants.10  
* **Rule 4: Guidance is Not a Legislative Rule** (administrative doctrine | well-established). Agency guidance (interpretive rules, policy statements, FAQs) lacks the force of law and cannot bind citizens or courts.1 Legislative rules, passed through notice-and-comment procedures, bind the public with statutory force.1 Treating guidance as a binding mandate without independent judicial review post-*Loper Bright* is a fatal failure of compliance planning.1  
* **Rule 5: Administrative Burden is Not Procedural Due Process** (public-administration practice | well-established). Administrative burdens (forms, delays, fee requirements) are operational costs that can cause rights underenforcement.1 Due process is the constitutional minimum of notice and a hearing required before a deprivation occurs.1 While a burden can undermine systemic legitimacy, it does not constitute a due process violation unless it rises to an absolute, unconstitutional barrier to access.1

## **Public Law as Authority Plus Constraint**

Public law is the structured framework governing the creation, distribution, exercise, and containment of state power.1 While private law facilitates commercial exchanges, property allocation, and tort compensation between formally equal actors 1, public law regulates the vertical, inherently unequal relationship between the sovereign state and the individual subject.1

The core of public law is a profound asymmetry (institutional design principle | well-established). In private ordering, parties enjoy broad freedom to execute any transaction or conduct unless explicitly prohibited by positive law.1 In public governance, however, the state and its officials possess no inherent freedom of action.1 Every projection of state coercion—including taxation, arrest, regulatory enforcement, benefit denial, and licensing—requires affirmative, positive authority from a recognized legal source.1

This public authority remains empty and illegitimate unless bounded by constitutional, statutory, procedural, and institutional constraints.1 Public law manages this tension by coupling state power with strict reason-giving duties, notice-and-comment participation, due process protections, and judicial review, transforming raw coercive force into legitimate administrative action.1

## **Source and Structure of Public Power**

Public power is not self-generating; it is an output of an organized, hierarchical system of authorization.1 Every exercise of public authority must trace its pedigree step-by-step up Hans Kelsen's pyramid of norms, ascending from the specific administrative action to the authorizing regulation, the enabling statute, and ultimately the foundational constitution.1

Public power is classified into three structural domains (constitutional rule | well-established):

1. **Inherent Power**: Powers intrinsic to sovereignty, such as a state's **police power** to regulate behaviors to protect public health, safety, morals, and the general welfare.1  
2. **Delegated Power**: Powers affirmatively transferred from a sovereign principal to an agent.1 Administrative agencies possess no inherent power; they derive their entire competence from enabling statutes enacted by the legislature.1  
3. **Implied Power**: Powers necessary and proper to execute an express delegation, although they cannot expand the core boundary of the statutory mandate.1

The threshold question in any public-law dispute is absolute: **Where did this actor get the power to do this?** If the state actor cannot point to an explicit statutory delegation or constitutional vesting of authority, their action is *ultra vires* and legally void from its inception, regardless of its social utility or political popularity.1

## **State Action and the Public/Private Boundary**

The state action doctrine is the structural gatekeeper of constitutional rights (constitutional rule | well-established). Because constitutions are designed vertically to protect individual liberty against state coercion, constitutional constraints—such as free speech, due process, and equal protection—apply strictly to actions taken by the state or its officials.1 Purely private conduct, no matter how powerful or economically dominant, remains exempt from these constitutional limits and is governed instead by private contract and tort law.1

To determine whether a private actor's conduct constitutes state action, courts apply four strict tests:

1. **The Public Function Test**: Applies when a private entity exercises powers traditionally and exclusively reserved to the sovereign, such as running a company town or conducting primary elections.1  
2. **The Joint Action / Nexus Test**: Applies when the state has so far insinuated itself into a position of interdependence with a private actor that they must be treated as joint participants in the challenged conduct.1  
3. **The Coercion / Significant Encouragement Test**: Applies when the state exercises such coercive influence, either overt or covert, that the private choice must be deemed that of the state.1  
4. **The Entwinement Test**: Applies when a private entity is structurally entwined with public officials and public funding, such as a state athletic association regulating public schools.1

This public/private boundary is a primary battlefield in platform governance. Multi-national social media platforms exercise de facto sovereign control over public communication and user access, yet they remain private actors protected by Section 230 and their own First Amendment editorial rights, insulating their content-moderation decisions from free-speech challenges unless direct government coercion is proven.32

## **Constitutional Structure and Separation of Powers**

The separation of powers is the foundational structural limit on the exercise of state coercion (constitutional rule | well-established). The Constitution divides public power horizontally among three branches to prevent the concentration of arbitrary authority, creating a system of checks and balances where each branch is restricted to its designated competence.1

Administrative agencies complicate this classical tripartite model by combining rulemaking (legislative), enforcement (executive), and adjudication (judicial) functions within a single institution.1 Public law manages this concentration of power through structural and appointment doctrines:

* **The Nondelegation Doctrine**: Prohibits Congress from delegating its core legislative power to executive agencies without providing an "intelligible principle" to guide and limit the agency's discretion.1  
* **The Appointments and Removal Power**: Governs the President's Article II authority to direct and supervise executive officers.27 In *Trump v. Slaughter* (pending 2026), the Supreme Court is directly reviewing whether the statutory removal protections for the Federal Trade Commission violate the separation of powers and whether *Humphrey's Executor* (1935) should be overruled, testing the very foundation of independent regulatory commissions.8  
* **The Unitary Executive Theory**: Asserts that Article II vests the entirety of the executive power in the President, requiring that all officers exercising executive authority must serve at the President's pleasure, which directly threatens the statutory "for-cause" protections of multi-member agencies.7

In *Trump v. Cook* (2026), this separation-of-powers clash extends to the Federal Reserve, where the Court must decide whether a President can remove a Federal Reserve Governor for cause based on pre-appointment conduct without prior notice or a hearing, balancing executive supervision with the monetary policy independence of the central bank.18

## **Federalism, Localism, and Vertical Allocation of Power**

Federalism is the vertical distribution of public authority across national, state, and local governments. In the United States, this vertical structure is anchored by the Supremacy Clause of Article VI, which establishes that valid federal laws, treaties, and regulations override conflicting state constitutions and statutes.1

This vertical allocation is governed by three primary doctrines:

1. **Preemption**: Federal law preempts state law either expressly (via explicit statutory language) or impliedly, which is subdivided into:  
   * *Conflict Preemption*: Where it is physically impossible to comply with both federal and state rules, or where the state rule stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment of federal objectives.1  
   * *Field Preemption*: Where the federal regulatory scheme is so pervasive that it is reasonable to infer Congress left no room for the states to supplement it.1  
2. **The Anti-Commandeering Doctrine**: Grounded in the Tenth Amendment, this doctrine prohibits the federal government from directly commanding state legislatures to pass specific laws, or state executive officers to enforce federal regulatory programs, preserving the states' independent political accountability.1  
3. **Cooperative Federalism and Conditional Spending**: Congress bypassed the anti-commandeering limit by using its Spending Clause power to condition federal grants on state compliance with federal policies.1 In *Landor v. Louisiana* (2025), the Supreme Court must evaluate this vertical conditional spending architecture, determining whether states that accept federal prison funds must submit to individual-capacity damages suits under the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA).17

At the local level, state constitutions distribute power to municipal governments through **Home Rule** charters, which delegate local police powers while keeping municipal actions subordinate to state-level preemption.1

## **Rights Enforcement Framework**

A constitutional or statutory civil right is not a self-executing slogan; it is an output of a highly structured procedural pipeline.1 To transform an abstract rights claim into a binding judicial remedy, the legal operator must execute the ten-gate Rights Enforcement Framework:

1. **Identify the Right or Protected Interest**: Locate the source of the right in a constitutional provision, federal civil-rights statute, state law, or administrative regulation.1  
2. **Identify the Government Actor or State Action**: Prove the defendant's conduct constitutes state action or was executed under color of law.1  
3. **Identify the Deprivation, Burden, Classification, Restriction, or Penalty**: Map the precise government classification, penalty, or restriction that burdens the right.1  
4. **Identify the Source of the Right**: Determine whether the right is constitutional, statutory, regulatory, treaty-based, or rooted in common law.1  
5. **Identify the Cause of Action or Procedural Vehicle**: Identify the statutory cause of action (such as 42 U.S.C. § 1983, RLUIPA, or the APA) that authorizes private enforcement; rights do not carry automatic private rights of action.3  
6. **Establish Standing, Ripeness, Exhaustion, and Forum Authority**: Satisfy Article III standing, ensure the claim is ripe, complete any mandatory administrative exhaustion, and confirm the forum has jurisdiction.3  
7. **Prove the Relevant Facts**: Reconstruct the historical record under the governing standard of proof.1  
8. **Apply the Appropriate Standard or Scrutiny**: Apply the applicable legal tier (rational basis, intermediate scrutiny, strict scrutiny, or arbitrary-and-capricious review).3  
9. **Overcome Immunity, Abstention, Remedial Limits, or Reviewability Barriers**: Defeat qualified immunity, official immunity, and sovereign immunity.3  
10. **Obtain a Remedy Capable of Correcting the Violation**: Secure an enforceable remedy, such as an injunction, damages, or vacatur, that successfully redresses the injury.3

## **Due Process as Legitimate Deprivation Architecture**

The Due Process Clauses of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments establish the legal system's discipline for turning state coercion into procedurally legitimate action (constitutional rule | well-established). The state cannot deprive any person of "life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." This protection is divided into two distinct doctrinal frameworks:

1. **Procedural Due Process**: Governs the timing and form of the required process, ensuring that before the state deprives an individual of a protected interest, it provides notice, a fair hearing, and a neutral decision-maker.3  
2. **Substantive Due Process**: Prohibits the state from infringing upon fundamental liberty and autonomy interests—regardless of the procedure provided—unless the restriction survives strict scrutiny, protecting unenumerated rights like bodily integrity, family association, and privacy.1

Procedural due process analysis requires a three-step inquiry:

* *Step 1: Protected Interest*: Has there been a deprivation of a constitutionally cognizable "liberty" or "property" interest? Property interests are not created by the Constitution; they are created by independent sources, such as state laws, municipal charters, or employment contracts that generate a legitimate claim of entitlement.1 In *Trump v. Cook* (2026), the due process battle turns on whether a Federal Reserve Governor holds a protected property interest in their 14-year term, entitling them to notice and a pre-termination hearing before removal.9  
* *Step 2: Deprivation*: Did government action directly cause the loss of this interest?  
* *Step 3: What Process is Due*: The court calibrates the required procedure by balancing the three factors established in *Mathews v. Eldridge*:

Process = f(Private Interest, Risk of Error and Value of Additional Safeguards, Government's Fiscal and Administrative Burden)

In algorithmic and data-driven benefits administration, this *Mathews* framework requires continuous, systematic evaluation to ensure that automated fraud detection and eligibility scoring do not result in unconstitutional, black-box deprivations of public assistance.11

## **Equal Protection and Anti-Classification Law**

The Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and the implied equal protection component of the Fifth Amendment limit the government's authority to classify individuals and treat similarly situated persons unequally (constitutional rule | well-established). Anti-classification law evaluates these distinctions through three rigid tiers of judicial scrutiny:

* **Strict Scrutiny**: Applies to suspect classifications (race, national origin, alienage) or laws burdening fundamental rights.1 The law must be narrowly tailored to achieve a compelling state interest.  
* **Intermediate Scrutiny**: Applies to quasi-suspect classifications (gender, illegitimacy).1 The law must be substantially related to an important government objective.  
* **Rational Basis**: Applies to economic or general classifications.1 The law must be rationally related to a legitimate government interest.

To establish unconstitutional discrimination, the plaintiff must prove both a **discriminatory effect** (unequal treatment relative to a comparable group) and a **discriminatory purpose** (that the government acted because of, not merely in spite of, its adverse effects on the protected group).1 This constitutional standard differs fundamentally from statutory anti-discrimination frameworks (such as Title VII, Title IX, or the Americans with Disabilities Act). Statutory regimes are broader, often prohibiting neutral practices that produce a **disparate impact** without requiring proof of discriminatory intent, and imposing affirmative duties of **reasonable accommodation** that go far beyond constitutional minimums.1

## **Civil Rights and Public-Law Causes of Action**

A civil-rights claim remains an unenforceable grievance unless carried by a valid procedural vehicle (statutory rule | well-established). Litigators must deploy specialized statutory causes of action to bring rights violations before a court:

* **42 U.S.C. § 1983**: The primary vehicle to enforce constitutional and statutory rights against state and local officials, allowing plaintiffs to seek damages against officials in their individual capacities, or injunctive relief against municipal entities under *Monell*.3  
* **The Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA)**: An express statutory cause of action protecting the religious rights of incarcerated persons.17 In *Landor v. Louisiana* (2025), the Supreme Court is deciding whether RLUIPA’s authorization of "appropriate relief against a government" includes individual-capacity damages suits, resolving a critical remedy gap for incarcerated religious minorities.17  
* **Pattern-or-Practice Enforcement**: Statutory authority granted to federal agencies (such as the DOJ Civil Rights Division) to sue municipal police departments, prisons, or school districts to reform systemic, structural violations of civil rights, typically culminating in long-term **consent decrees** monitored by independent federal auditors.3

These public-law claims must be strictly distinguished from private tort and contract claims. While a private tort focuses on individual compensation, public-law litigation targets systemic accountability, often utilizing **class actions** and **fee-shifting** statutes (like 42 U.S.C. § 1988) to alter the economic leverage of litigation and force structural reforms.3

## **Administrative Governance Operating Model**

Modern governance is executed primarily through administrative agencies, which act as institutional hybrids combining legislative, executive, and judicial powers under statutory and constitutional constraints (administrative doctrine | well-established). Agencies operate through three primary functional domains:

1. **Rulemaking**: The administrative equivalent of legislation, where agencies draft and promulgate **legislative rules** (which carry the force of law) or **non-legislative rules** (interpretive rules and policy statements).1  
2. **Enforcement**: The executive function of investigating, inspecting, and prosecuting regulatory non-compliance.1 This domain is governed by **enforcement discretion**, allowing agencies to allocate resources, select targets, and negotiate settlements.1  
3. **Adjudication**: The judicial function of resolving regulatory disputes and imposing penalties in internal agency tribunals overseen by **Administrative Law Judges (ALJs)**.1

Every agency action must remain strictly within the boundaries of the competence delegated by the legislature in its enabling statute; any action exceeding these boundaries is *ultra vires* and legally void.1

## **Rulemaking Architecture**

Rulemaking is the process by which administrative agencies formulate, amend, or repeal regulations. Under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), the default mechanism is **notice-and-comment rulemaking** under Section 553 (statutory rule | well-established):

* **The Notice Stage**: The agency must publish a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) in the Federal Register, detailing the legal authority, the terms of the proposed rule, and the scientific or technical data supporting it.  
* **The Comment Stage**: The agency must provide the public with a meaningful opportunity to submit written data, views, or arguments.  
* **The Final Rule Stage**: The agency must publish the final rule accompanied by a **statement of basis and purpose** that actively responds to all significant public comments.

Rulemaking validity requires compiling a robust **administrative record** that survives "hard look" judicial review.1 The agency must demonstrate that it examined the relevant data, considered significant alternatives, and articulated a rational connection between the facts found and the choice made.1 In *Bradley v. DentalPlans.com* (2026), this rulemaking boundary was tested under the Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA), where the court held that because the TCPA did not explicitly delegate to the FCC the authority to require written consent, the agency's attempt to impose a heightened written-consent requirement via rulemaking exceeded its statutory authority.44

## **Guidance, Soft Law, and Practical Coercion**

Agencies routinely bypass the expensive, time-consuming notice-and-comment process by issuing **non-legislative rules**, commonly referred to as **guidance**, **soft law**, or **interpretive rules** (agency practice | well-established). While formal legislative regulations carry the force of law and bind reviewing courts, agency guidance is formally non-binding and possesses only persuasive authority.1

Despite their lack of formal legal force, guidance documents (such as FAQs, policy statements, enforcement manuals, and compliance bulletins) exercise immense **practical coercion** over regulated industries.1 A business confronted with an aggressive agency "compliance FAQ" cannot simply ignore it; the guidance signals the agency's enforcement priorities, and non-compliance invites immediate audits, investigations, and reputational exposure.1 Following *Loper Bright* (2024), courts must independently evaluate these agency materials, refusing to defer to guidance interpretations of ambiguous statutes and instead assigning them only *Skidmore* respect proportional to their thoroughness, consistency, and logical persuasiveness.1

## **Administrative Adjudication and Agency Factfinding**

Administrative adjudication is the process by which agencies resolve regulatory disputes, license applications, and enforce penalties through internal tribunals (administrative doctrine | well-established). This process is divided into two primary procedural models:

1. **Formal Adjudication**: Under Sections 554, 556, and 557 of the APA, this model requires a trial-like hearing before an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ), a formal record, witness cross-examination, and a decision based strictly on substantial evidence.1  
2. **Informal Adjudication**: A residual category covering the vast majority of agency actions, requiring only basic procedural due process (notice and an opportunity to present a response).3

Factfinding in administrative proceedings is highly specialized. Unlike Article III courts, agencies are not bound by the strict Federal Rules of Evidence; they may admit hearsay and rely heavily on technical and scientific **agency expertise**.1 When an agency's factual findings are challenged on judicial review, courts apply the highly deferential **substantial evidence standard**, upholding the agency's findings of fact if supported by "more than a mere scintilla"—meaning such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support the conclusion.1 This adjudicative power has been sharply curtailed by the Supreme Court. In *SEC v. Jarkesy* (2024), the Court held that the Seventh Amendment requires a jury trial in an Article III federal court, rather than an in-house ALJ tribunal, when an agency seeks civil penalties for claims akin to common-law fraud, fundamentally altering the enforcement landscape for independent agencies.1

## **Enforcement Discretion and Public Enforcement Strategy**

Enforcement discretion is the power of executive agencies and prosecutors to decide whether to investigate, prosecute, or settle regulatory violations (administrative doctrine | well-established). Under *Heckler v. Chaney* (1985), an agency's decision *not* to enforce is presumptively committed to agency discretion by law and is unreviewable by courts.3

This immense discretion is the core driver of public enforcement strategy:

* **Selective and Prioritized Enforcement**: Agencies must allocate limited resources, prioritizing systemic or high-profile violations while permitting minor non-compliance to persist, creating a public-law tension between efficiency and the risk of discriminatory or arbitrary enforcement.1  
* **Cooperation Credit and Self-Reporting**: Regulatory enforcement is increasingly data-driven. The DOJ's Corporate Enforcement Policy actively incentivizes voluntary self-reporting. Under the DOJ Corporate Whistleblower Awards Pilot Program (2025), a company can secure a presumption of a non-prosecution declination if it self-reports financial or corrupt misconduct within 120 days of receiving an internal whistleblower report, transforming enforcement from an adversarial trial to a race to disclose.45  
* **Monitorships and Consent Orders**: Settlements of complex regulatory violations routinely culminate in deferred prosecution agreements (DPAs) or consent orders that impose independent, third-party **compliance monitors** to supervise the corporation's internal operations, acting as private-public hybrid governance structures.1

## **Judicial Review of Public Action**

Judicial review is the structured interface where Article III courts evaluate the lawfulness, rationality, and constitutionality of public actions on a closed administrative record (remedial doctrine | well-established). To secure judicial review under the APA, a plaintiff must satisfy four threshold gates:

1. **Standing**: Demonstrate Article III standing.3  
2. **Finality**: Challenge only "final agency action" under Section 704.3  
3. **Exhaustion**: Complete all contractually or statutorily mandated administrative remedies.3  
4. **Ripeness**: Prove the dispute has crystallized and is fit for judicial resolution.3

Review is conducted strictly on the **administrative record**, which includes all documents and data considered by the agency at the moment the decision was made; extra-record evidence is generally inadmissible.1 If the court finds the agency action unlawful, Section 706 of the APA commands that the court "hold unlawful and set aside" the action.23 For decades, "set aside" was understood to authorize **universal vacatur**—the total nullification of the rule as to all parties nationwide.48 In *Trump v. CASA* (2025), the Court limited federal courts' authority to issue "universal injunctions" under the Judiciary Act of 1789, sparking an intense, ongoing debate over whether Section 706 of the APA still permits universal vacatur of agency rules or whether relief must be strictly limited to the individual plaintiffs in the case.20

## **Administrative Deference, Respect, and Judicial Judgment**

The landscape of administrative law underwent a historic, structural paradigm shift in 2024. For forty years under *Chevron U.S.A. v. NRDC* (1984), federal courts deferred to an agency's reasonable interpretation of ambiguous statutory language, heavily tilting the balance of power toward the administrative state.1

In *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (2024), the Supreme Court explicitly overruled *Chevron*, holding that the APA requires Article III courts to exercise their independent judgment to find the single "best" meaning of a statute.1 Following *Loper Bright*, courts evaluate agency interpretations under several distinct frameworks:

* **Skidmore Respect**: An agency's statutory interpretation receives no binding deference, but is given "respect" proportional to its thoroughness, logical persuasiveness, and consistency with prior pronouncements.1  
* **The Major Questions Doctrine**: If an agency asserts authority over a matter of "vast economic and political significance," the court will not find delegation in ambiguous text; the agency must point to explicit, clear congressional authorization.1 In *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (2026), the Court applied the Major Questions Doctrine to strike down sweeping tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), reaffirming that even emergency executive actions cannot bypass clear statutory limits.6  
* **Regulatory Interpretation (Kisor/Auer)**: While statutory deference is dead, courts still afford a level of deference to an agency's interpretation of its own genuinely ambiguous regulations under *Kisor v. Wilkie* (2019), provided the agency's reading is authoritative, consistent, and reflects specialized expertise.1

## **Public-Law Remedies and Barriers**

Public-law remedies translate constitutional and statutory victories from paper declarations into coercive reality, but they are strictly constrained by a complex lattice of immunities and procedural barriers (remedial doctrine | well-established). These remedies and barriers include:

* **Sovereign Immunity**: Under state sovereign immunity and the Eleventh Amendment, non-consenting states cannot be sued in federal or state courts by private parties for monetary damages.3 This bar does not apply to counties or municipalities, which can be held liable under *Monell* if the rights violation was caused by an official municipal policy, custom, or practice.3 In *Galette v. New Jersey Transit Corp.* (2026), the Supreme Court clarified that state-created corporate entities are not entitled to Eleventh Amendment immunity if they are structured as distinct legal persons responsible for their own obligations, limiting the "arm-of-the-state" defense.3  
* **Qualified Immunity**: Protects government officials from individual civil liability under Section 1983 unless their conduct violated a "clearly established" constitutional or statutory right.3 To defeat qualified immunity, a civil-rights plaintiff must identify a binding Supreme Court or circuit precedent with a nearly identical factual matrix to prove the right was clearly established.16  
* **Official Immunity**: Absolute immunity shields specific public actors (including judges, prosecutors, and legislators) from civil liability for actions taken within their official roles to preserve the independence of their offices.1  
* **Universal Injunction Curtailment**: Following *Trump v. CASA* (2025), federal district courts lack equitable authority to issue nationwide or universal injunctions that benefit non-parties to the litigation.20 Preliminary and permanent injunctions must be strictly tailored to provide complete relief to the specific plaintiffs with standing before the court.21

## **Public-Law Litigation Strategy and Institutional Posture**

Public-law litigation differs fundamentally from ordinary private disputes. Private disputes typically focus on bilateral economic compensation on a closed record.1 Public-law cases, conversely, are high-stakes struggles over institutional authority, public policy, and constitutional legitimacy.1

Litigators must manage several strategic variables (strategic litigation consideration | well-established):

* **The Government Defendant Posture**: Government defendants are "repeat players" possessing deep structural advantages: limitless legal resources, the capacity to delay proceedings to exhaust resource-strapped plaintiffs, and the ability to unilaterally moot a case by changing their policies mid-litigation to avoid adverse precedent.3  
* **Record-Building in Administrative Challenges**: Unlike private litigation where discovery is broad under Rule 26, administrative record challenges are generally restricted to the documents considered by the agency.3 Litigators must focus their strategy on proving the record is incomplete, or that the agency relied on extra-record factors, to force a **remand**.1  
* **The Impact of *Trump v. CASA***: Because universal preliminary injunctions are now unavailable, public-interest plaintiffs can no longer secure immediate, nationwide relief for non-parties.20 This places immense pressure on plaintiffs' counsel to seek formal **Rule 23 class certification** early in the litigation, or coordinate parallel, multi-forum "test cases" to build national momentum.20

## **Emergency Powers, National Security, and Crisis Governance**

Emergencies present the most acute challenge to the rule of law, testing the boundaries between executive speed, scientific expertise, and constitutional constraints (constitutional rule | well-established). During a public-health pandemic, natural disaster, economic collapse, or foreign-policy threat, executive branches routinely invoke emergency statutes (such as the Stafford Act, the Public Health Service Act, or the International Emergency Economic Powers Act) to centralize authority and suspend ordinary legislative and procedural requirements.6

Public law handles emergencies through two primary doctrinal models:

1. **The Normalization Model**: Asserts that the Constitution does not bend during crises; emergency actions must trace their authority to a valid source and submit to standard judicial review.1 In *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (2026), the Supreme Court struck down sweeping emergency tariffs, holding that even IEEPA’s broad authority does not permit a President to bypass the legislative power to impose taxes.6  
2. **The Deference Model**: Historically, courts deferred to executive branch judgments during national security crises, accepting restrictions on liberties that would be unconstitutional in peacetime.1

To prevent the normalization of exceptional power, courts must enforce **temporariness** constraints, actively reviewing whether the factual and scientific evidence supports the emergency showing, and requiring the state to revert to ordinary constitutional procedures the moment the immediate crisis recedes.1

## **Transparency, Participation, and Accountability**

Public governance derives its legitimacy from the consent of the governed, which is operationalized through structural mechanisms of transparency, participation, and accountability (public-administration practice | well-established). These mechanisms act as a system of checks and balances to prevent arbitrary bureaucratic expansion:

* **Freedom of Information Regimes (FOIA)**: Statutory frameworks allowing citizens to inspect internal agency records, which is the primary tool to uncover regulatory capture, administrative bias, and procedural defaults.1  
* **Notice-and-Comment Participation**: Ensures that regulated parties and public stakeholders can shape administrative rules before they carry the coercive force of law.1  
* **Inspector General Oversight and Audit Offices**: Independent, internal agency watchdog offices that audit compliance with statutory mandates, financial integrity, and whistleblower protections.5  
* **Democratic Accountability**: The ultimate check on the executive branch through elections.1 However, this democratic connection must not be co-opted to justify the arbitrary firing of career civil servants and scientific experts.54

These transparency systems impose significant **administrative burdens** and operational costs.1 A public-law system must continually balance the need for open, participatory governance with the executive branch's duty to deliver efficient, functional public services.1

## **Algorithmic, Data-Driven, and AI-Assisted Governance**

Public agencies are increasingly delegating critical decision-making—including welfare benefit allocations, risk scoring for bail, policing resource distribution, immigration eligibility, and regulatory enforcement priorities—to automated, data-driven machine learning algorithms (emerging | contested). This algorithmic governance is opaque, creating a "black box" that challenges the core principles of procedural due process.43

To preserve constitutional values, algorithmic public administration must submit to five public-law requirements:

1. **Notice and Transparency**: The deployer must provide "clear and conspicuous notice" that covered automated decision-making technology (ADMT) is being used to materially influence a consequential decision.42  
2. **The Right to Explanation and Contestability**: If an adverse decision is made, the target must receive a plain-language explanation of the decision's basis 42 and have the right to request a meaningful human re-review.47  
3. **Data Quality and Auditability**: Agencies must maintain audit trails of the training datasets and software parameters to allow courts to review the system's accuracy and detect indirect bias.47  
4. **No Automated Default**: Human reviewers must be trained and prohibited from blindly defaulting to the system's output, preserving human discretion and accountability.47  
5. **State Legislative Guardrails**: In SB 26-189 (the "Restated AIA" passed May 12, 2026), Colorado became a model for state-level ADMT regulation, replacing its original, complex "algorithmic discrimination" framework with a notice- and disclosure-focused regime that defines deployer and developer liability based on relative responsibility, showing how public law must evolve to govern digital power.47

## **Public Procurement, Spending, and Conditional Governance**

The government projects immense authority not only through direct regulation, but also through its role as a market participant, utilizing contracts, grants, and spending conditions to govern private conduct (statutory rule | well-established). This spending-power governance is highly effective, allowing the state to impose social, economic, and compliance mandates that would be unconstitutional if enacted as direct regulatory prohibitions.2

This spending-power authority is governed by three primary limits:

1. **The Unconstitutional Conditions Doctrine**: Prohibits the government from conditioning a public benefit, grant, or contract on the recipient surrendering a constitutionally protected right, such as requiring a contractor to sign a pledge waiving their First Amendment right to criticize a government policy.2  
2. **The Spending Power Limits**: Under *South Dakota v. Dole* (1987), federal spending conditions must be clear, unambiguous, related to the federal interest in the national program, and must not rise to the level of "coercion" that practically destroys state sovereign choice.  
3. **Contractor Accountability**: Government contracts are private instruments embedded in public-law constraints.2 In *GEO Group v. Menocal* (2026), the Supreme Court held that a private contractor managing a federal immigration detention facility was not entitled to derivative sovereign immunity under *Yearsley* for claims of forced labor, demonstrating that private entities performing delegated public functions face direct civil liability if they violate statutory limits.3

## **Public Employment, Education, Benefits, and Licensing**

Public law governs state-created relationships, statuses, and entitlements, transforming what classical law treated as mere "privileges" into legally protected "rights" subject to constitutional due process (constitutional rule | well-established):

* **Public Employment**: Career civil servants who can only be fired "for cause" hold a protected property interest in their employment, entitling them to notice and a pre-termination hearing under *Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill* (1985).1  
* **Public Education**: Students in public schools possess First Amendment speech rights (which cannot be suppressed unless they cause a material and substantial disruption under *Tinker*) and Fourteenth Amendment due process rights (requiring notice and a hearing before suspension).1  
* **Welfare and Public Benefits**: In *Goldberg v. Kelly* (1970), the Supreme Court established that public assistance benefits constitute a form of property, requiring a full, pre-termination evidentiary hearing before benefits can be suspended.1  
* **Professional Licensing**: A state-issued professional license (e.g., medical, legal, or commercial) constitutes a protected property interest.1 The state cannot revoke or suspend a license without providing notice of the alleged violations, an opportunity to inspect the evidence, and a hearing before a neutral board.1

## **Public Law Under Inequality and Administrative Burden**

While public law is designed to protect individual rights and ensure institutional accountability, it can also function as a tool of systemic exclusion. Administrative systems frequently allocate public benefits, licenses, and legal protections through complex procedural networks that impose severe **administrative burdens** on vulnerable populations (public-administration practice | well-established).

These burdens operate across three dimensions:

1. **Compliance Costs**: The time, effort, and expense required to complete complex forms, secure official documents, and navigate digital portals.1  
2. **Learning Costs**: The cognitive effort required to understand complex eligibility rules, deadlines, and legal procedures in an opaque administrative state.1  
3. **Psychological Costs**: The stress, loss of dignity, and fear of hostile surveillance experienced by low-income, disabled, or immigrant individuals when interacting with state caseworkers and automated eligibility systems.1

This administrative burden results in **rights underenforcement**, where eligible individuals forfeit their benefits or accept unlawful state action because they cannot afford the "procedural tax" required to challenge it.1 Public law must continually balance the state's interest in preventing fraud with its constitutional duty to ensure equal, dignified, and low-barrier access to justice.1

## **Comparative Public Law and Administrative State Variants**

Public law and administrative governance are organized through diverse institutional architectures across global legal systems (comparative observation | jurisdiction-dependent):

* **Common-Law Constitutionalism**: Organizes judicial review through ordinary, generalist courts of law (e.g., the United States and the United Kingdom), where a single supreme court acts as the ultimate arbiter of both private disputes and constitutional limits.1  
* **Civil-Law Administrative Courts**: Separates public-law disputes from ordinary civil litigation, routing all challenges to state action through specialized, independent administrative courts (such as France's *Conseil d'État* or Germany's *Bundesverwaltungsgericht*) that apply a specialized, code-based body of public law.1  
* **Parliamentary Sovereignty**: The dominant model in the United Kingdom, where Parliament holds supreme legislative authority and courts cannot invalidate primary statutes as unconstitutional, restricting judicial review to ensuring that executive branch actions remain within statutory limits.  
* **The Proportionality Model**: The dominant framework in European and international human-rights tribunals, which replaces the rigid "tiers of scrutiny" used in U.S. law with a flexible, four-step proportionality test to determine if a government restriction on a right is necessary and proportionate to achieve a legitimate public objective.1

## **Public Law Failure Modes**

Public-law systems collapse into illegitimacy not because they lack rules, but because their rules are co-opted, bypassed, or rendered operationally hollow. Legal operators must dynamically diagnose and repair these primary failure modes of public governance:

* **Statutory Ultra Vires**: An agency or official acting entirely outside their statutory competence.1  
* **Deficient Procedure**: Promulgating legislative rules without notice and comment, or executing deprivations without notice and a hearing.1  
* **Arbitrary Reasoning**: Issuing a regulatory decision without a rational connection between the facts found and the choice made.1  
* **Weak Record**: Relying on speculative assumptions or inadequate evidence that fails the substantial-evidence standard.1  
* **State Action Boundary Collapse**: Permitting private entities to exercise sovereign-like power over speech or data without constitutional constraints.1  
* **Sovereign Immunity Barriers**: State-created corporate entities improperly claiming Eleventh Amendment immunity to block damages.3  
* **Qualified Immunity Shield**: Civil-rights claims defeated because no identical factual precedent exists to "clearly establish" the right.3  
* **Overbroad Executive Removal**: Purporting to fire independent agency commissioners or Fed Governors without satisfying statutory cause.8  
* **Major Questions Violation**: Bypassing Congress to regulate vast economic sectors based on vague, historical statutory text.1  
* **Algorithmic Due Process Black Box**: Delegating benefits eligibility or risk scoring to proprietary, un-auditable AI models.11  
* **Selective and Discriminatory Enforcement**: Exercising enforcement discretion in an inconsistent, racially biased, or politically vindictive manner.1  
* **Decorative Compliance Systems**: Building complex, paper-only corporate compliance policies that fail to prevent or detect actual misconduct.5

## **Worked Examples**

This section analyzes fifteen distinct, stage-sensitive scenarios to demonstrate the operational application of public-law principles under conditions of administrative and constitutional pressure.

### **Worked Example 1: Agency Rulemaking Struck Down as Statutory Ultra Vires**

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) revamped its premerger notification program under its new Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) rule, which went into effect on February 10, 2025, expanding the categories of information that parties needed to disclose before closing transactions.14 A month before the new rule took effect, business groups, including the Chamber of Commerce of the United States, challenged the FTC's statutory authority under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) in a federal district court in Texas.14 Under Section 18a(d)(1) of the HSR Act, the FTC is empowered to require premerger notifications containing "documentary material and information... necessary and appropriate" to enable antitrust enforcement.14 On February 12, 2026, Judge Jeremy D. Kernodle of the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Texas vacated the rule universally, holding that the agency exceeded its statutory authority because it failed to demonstrate that the new rule's massive compliance costs were reasonably outweighed by its benefits, and that the rule was arbitrary and capricious for the same reason.14

### **Worked Example 2: Property Entitlement Protected by Pre-Termination Hearing**

Lisa D. Cook was nominated by President Biden in 2023 to a full 14-year term on the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System and confirmed by the Senate.9 In August 2025, President Donald Trump issued a public letter removing Governor Cook from office "for cause," citing allegations that, prior to her appointment, she had misrepresented her residency on a mortgage application to obtain favorable loan terms.9 The President's letter did not afford Cook any prior notice or opportunity to respond.9 Cook filed suit in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia, alleging that her removal violated the Federal Reserve Act's requirement that Governors may be removed only "for cause" and that the President's unilateral action violated her procedural due process rights under the Fifth Amendment.9 Judge Jia Cobb granted a preliminary injunction, holding that Cook was substantially likely to succeed because a statutory 14-year term creates a constitutionally protected property interest, which cannot be deprived without prior notice and an opportunity to be heard.41

### **Worked Example 3: Prison Grooming and the Form of Hearing**

Damon Landor, a devout Rastafarian serving a short sentence in a Louisiana state prison, wore his hair in dreadlocks in accordance with his religious beliefs.17 Near the end of his sentence, Landor was transferred to the Raymond Laborde Correctional Center, which maintained a strict hair-cutting policy.17 Upon arrival, Landor explained his religious practice and presented the intake officers with a printout of a Fifth Circuit decision holding that RLUIPA protected his right to keep his hair long.17 The intake guards threw the decision into the trash, handcuffed Landor to a chair, and forcibly shaved his head bald.17 Landor sued under RLUIPA seeking individual damages.17 The district court and the Fifth Circuit dismissed the damages claim, holding that RLUIPA does not authorize individual-capacity damages suits.17 The Supreme Court granted certiorari (*Landor v. Louisiana*, No. 23-1197), and during oral arguments on November 10, 2025, the Justices focused on the notice requirements of Spending Clause legislation, analyzing whether states accepting federal funds had clear, un-waived notice that their employees could be held personally liable for damages if they violated RLUIPA's statutory mandates.36

### **Worked Example 4: Intermediate Scrutiny and the Transgender Sports Ban**

West Virginia passed a statute categorically banning transgender girls from participating on girls' athletic teams, designating school sports teams strictly based on biological sex determined at birth.15 B.P.J., a young transgender girl who wished to run on her school's track team, brought a lawsuit challenging the ban as a violation of the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause.15 Under established equal-protection doctrine, sex-based classifications are subject to intermediate scrutiny, placing the burden on the state to prove that the classification is substantially related to an important government objective.15 The state justified the ban as necessary to protect athletic fairness and safety for biological females.15 B.P.J. argued that the categorical ban is overbroad and lacks intermediate fit because it excludes transgender girls who have undergone puberty-blocking treatments and have no athletic advantage, demonstrating that the classification fails intermediate scrutiny fits.15 The case reached the Supreme Court in *West Virginia v. B.P.J.*, directly testing intermediate scrutiny limits in public school athletics.15

### **Worked Example 5: State Action and the Social Media Public Forum**

Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez blocked a constituent from her personal @AOC Twitter account on the basis of their viewpoints, prompting a First Amendment lawsuit.40 Under the state action doctrine, constitutional free speech protections apply strictly to government action, not to private conduct.30 Following *Lindke v. Freed* (2024), a public official's social media activity constitutes state action only if the official possessed actual authority to speak on the state's behalf and purported to exercise that authority.39 In parallel litigation, the Eighth Circuit in *Campbell v. Reisch* held that a representative’s Twitter account was not an official government account because it focused on her political campaign rather than her legislative duties, and thus she was not acting under color of state law when blocking a constituent.40

### **Worked Example 6: Notice-and-Comment Rule Challenged for Cost-Benefit Deficit**

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) approved its new premerger notification rule under the Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) Act, expanding the categories of documents and information that corporate filing parties must disclose.14 A month before the rule's implementation, the Chamber of Commerce challenged the rule under Section 706 of the APA.14 The plaintiffs argued that the FTC's cost-benefit analysis was structurally deficient because the agency did not prove that the rule's significant and widespread operational costs on businesses were "necessary and appropriate" to carry out the antitrust laws.14 On February 12, 2026, the Eastern District of Texas agreed, finding the rule arbitrary and capricious and vacating it universally, demonstrating that agencies must provide a rational, record-based connection justifying cost-benefit trade-offs during rulemaking.14

### **Worked Example 7: Interpretive Guidance Treated as Practically Coercive**

The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) issued a compliance manual and informal FAQs asserting that the Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) requires businesses to obtain "written" consent before placing automated telemarketing calls.44 Although the TCPA's statutory text refers only to "prior express consent," regulated companies treated the FCC's written-consent guidance as practically coercive to avoid multi-million dollar class-action statutory penalties.44 Post-*Loper Bright*, a federal district court in Maryland in *Bradley v. DentalPlans.com* (2026) rejected the FCC's written-consent requirement, holding that because the TCPA does not delegate to the FCC the authority to interpret the statute, the court owed zero deference to the agency's guidance and concluded that the plain statutory term "consent" did not authorize the agency to impose a heightened written-consent mandate.44

### **Worked Example 8: Enforcement Discretion and the Selective Removals Dispute**

DHS issued new guidance governing the removal of final-order aliens to third countries, establishing that before removal, DHS must determine whether that country has provided diplomatic assurances that the alien will not be persecuted or tortured.13 Plaintiffs sued on behalf of a class, alleging that DHS violated the INA and the Due Process Clause by removing aliens without providing prior written notice of the third country or an opportunity to assert a fear of persecution.13 The district court entered final relief vacating DHS's policy and entering a declaratory judgment.13 On appeal in *DHS v. D.V.D.* (2025), the Supreme Court stayed the district court's universal vacatur, demonstrating that while agencies possess enforcement discretion over resource allocation, their policies must remain within statutory and constitutional boundaries.13

### **Worked Example 9: Civil-Rights Claim Defeated by Qualified Immunity**

An arrestee, Johnson, brought excessive force claims under Section 1983 against police officers Salter and Noble, alleging that Salter's strikes to his head and Noble's deployment of a Taser drive stun violated his Fourth Amendment rights.52 The officers asserted qualified immunity.52 Under established doctrine, once qualified immunity is raised, the burden of proof shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate that the officers' conduct violated a constitutional right, and that the right was "clearly established" at the time.52 In *Johnson v. Salter* (5th Cir. 2026), the Fifth Circuit held that the officers were entitled to qualified immunity because the plaintiff failed to identify any binding Supreme Court or Fifth Circuit precedent with a similar factual matrix that clearly established that head strikes or a Taser drive stun were unconstitutional under those specific arrest conditions.52

### **Worked Example 10: High-Grievance Plaintiff Defeated by Class Standing**

An consumer plaintiff filed a class-action lawsuit against a medical diagnostic provider, alleging statutory civil-rights or privacy violations.3 The district court certified a massive national damages class under Rule 23(b)(3).3 On appeal, the defendant argued that the class must be decertified because it included thousands of "uninjured" class members who lacked Article III standing under *TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez*, which requires a concrete, real-world injury.3 The Second and Eighth Circuits hold that a class cannot be certified if it includes uninjured members, while the Ninth and Eleventh Circuits permit such classes.3 The Supreme Court granted certiorari in *Laboratory Corporation of America Holdings v. Davis* (2025) to resolve the split, but ultimately dismissed the writ as improvidently granted (DIG), leaving the plaintiff's overbroad class-action victory in limbo and demonstrating how standing requirements act as fatal procedural barriers.58

### **Worked Example 11: Judicial Review Ending in Remand without Immediate Merits Victory**

A federal district court in Texas reviewed a final agency rule promulgated by the Department of Education under the Administrative Procedure Act.58 The court concluded that the agency's statement of basis and purpose was procedurally deficient because the agency failed to consider a significant, less-costly alternative raised in the public comments.14 Although the plaintiff demanded that the court immediately declare their statutory right to exemption, the court applied Section 706 of the APA and entered a remedy of **remand** to the agency without vacatur.25 This remedy returns the rule to the agency to cure its reasoning deficit without invalidating the rule during the interim, demonstrating that judicial review often results in a procedural reset rather than an immediate substantive victory.25

### **Worked Example 12: Independent Construction post-Chevron**

A business group challenged a Department of Labor regulation altering the minimum salary threshold for overtime exemptions under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), arguing the agency's threshold exceeded the statutory definition of "executive, administrative, or professional" employees.1 Previously under the *Chevron* doctrine, the court would have deferred to the agency's reasonable interpretation of the ambiguous statutory terms.1 Post-2024 under *Loper Bright*, the court exercised its independent judgment to determine the "best reading" of the FLSA.1 In *Mayfield v. DOL* (5th Cir. 2024), the court verified the boundary of the statutory delegation and evaluated the agency's interpretation under *Skidmore* respect, illustrating that agency expertise is treated as a persuasive factor rather than a controlling mandate.1

### **Worked Example 13: Emergency Tariff Order Blocked Under IEEPA**

President Trump issued Executive Order 14160 declaring a national emergency and invoking the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to impose sweeping tariffs on all foreign imports.6 Import-export firms challenged the order, arguing that IEEPA authorizes the President to "regulate, prevent, or prohibit" property transactions, but does not explicitly delegate the power to impose tariffs or taxes.6 In *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* (2026), the Supreme Court struck down the tariffs, holding that the power to impose taxes belongs exclusively to Congress under Article I, and that the President cannot use vague, emergency statutory language to claim vast economic lawmaking power without clear congressional authorization.6

### **Worked Example 14: Algorithmic Benefits Denial Challenged for Lack of Explanation**

A state social-safety agency deployed an automated machine-learning algorithm to detect unemployment benefit fraud, resulting in the sudden, automated suspension of benefits for thousands of recipients.11 A recipient, challenged the suspension as a violation of procedural due process.11 Applying the *Mathews v. Eldridge* balancing test, the court held that the automated deprivation was unconstitutional because the agency failed to provide the recipient with a clear notice of the specific data variables used by the algorithm to flag the file, and denied them a meaningful opportunity to contest the "black-box" decision before a human hearing officer, showing how algorithmic governance must conform to due process requirements.11

### **Worked Example 15: Public Procurement Conditions Governing Private DEI Conduct**

The federal government conditioned its research grants to universities on the recipients implementing specific diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) compliance reporting, relying on its Spending Clause power.2 A university challenged the conditions as an unconstitutional expansion of federal authority into private-sector operations.59 The court rejected the challenge, holding that the Spending Clause authorizes Congress to attach unambiguous conditions to federal funds to promote the general welfare, provided the conditions are not unconstitutionally coercive, demonstrating how the government can contractually govern private conduct through its spending power.2

## **LLM Public Law Discipline**

To prevent the catastrophic failures of legal reasoning documented in modern case law—including Rule 11 sanctions, suspended practice privileges, and class representative disqualifications 3—both human legal operators and generative AI systems must enforce thirteen strict anti-hallucination **LLM Public Law Discipline Heuristics**:

1. **Never Analyze Public Action without the Governing Cadence**: Prompt models to identify the exact actor, source of authority, affected interest, procedure used, discretion exercised, constraints, review path, standard of review, and remedy.1  
2. **Never Treat a Constitutional Slogan as a Complete Rights Analysis**: Treat rights not as abstract declarations, but as outputs of a ten-gate procedural pipeline requiring a cause of action, standing, and the overcoming of immunities.1  
3. **Never Treat Agency Guidance as Binding Law**: Post-*Loper Bright*, force models to analyze guidance as formally non-binding soft law subject to *Skidmore* respect, never as a binding legislative rule.1  
4. **Never Collapse Statutory Authority, Constitutional Limits, and Policy**: Isolate whether a public actor's conduct is blocked because it is *ultra vires* (statutory), unconstitutional (constitutional), or merely an abuse of discretion (reason-giving).1  
5. **Never Ignore Procedural Hurdles**: Prior to evaluating the substantive merits, force models to audit standing, ripeness, mootness, finality, and administrative exhaustion.3  
6. **Never Discuss Due Process without a Protected Interest**: Prohibit models from analyzing due process violations without first identifying the specific state law, contract, or charter creating a protected "liberty" or "property" interest.1  
7. **Never Discuss Equal Protection without its Core Elements**: Prohibit models from summarizing discrimination claims without identifying the classification, the comparable group, the tier of scrutiny, and the evidence of both discriminatory effect and purpose.1  
8. **Never Assume a Right Has an Automatic Damages Remedy**: Explicitly verify whether the governing statute authorizes individual damages, or whether the claim is barred by Eleventh Amendment sovereign immunity or qualified immunity.3  
9. **Never Assume Unlawful Action Produces a Universal Remedy**: Post-*CASA*, restrict prospective injunctive relief strictly to the named plaintiffs unless a Rule 23 class is certified, while analyzing Section 706 APA claims for universal vacatur.20  
10. **Never Analyze Public Law as Pure Politics in Legal Costume**: Enforce strict doctrinal discipline; every public-law challenge must be framed around statutory text, administrative records, standards of review, and jurisdictional rules.1  
11. **Never Average Jurisdictional Splits**: If federal circuits or state supreme courts divide on a public-law question (such as class-standing or agency removal), preserve and map the split geographically; do not synthesize a false national consensus.1  
12. **Mandate Live Citator Validation**: Prohibit the citation of any constitutional or administrative precedent without passing the authority through a live citator to verify subsequent treatment and detect red or yellow flags.1  
13. **Isolate Holdings from Dicta in Case Summaries**: Prompt models to decompose public-law opinions, separating the binding *ratio decidendi* (necessary to resolve the material facts) from non-binding *obiter dicta*, conurrences, or dissents.1

## **Key Takeaways**

* **Public Law is the Discipline of Lawful Coercion**: Unlike private parties who are free to act unless prohibited, public actors require affirmative statutory or constitutional authority and must act within procedural limits.1  
* ***Loper Bright*** **Reclaims Judicial Judgment**: Overruling *Chevron*, the Supreme Court established that courts must exercise independent judgment to find the best meaning of a statute, leaving agencies with only *Skidmore* respect.1  
* **The Major Questions Doctrine is a Structural Veto**: Agencies cannot regulate vast economic or political sectors without explicit, clear congressional authorization (*Learning Resources* 2026).1  
* ***Trump v. CASA*** **Restricts Universal Relief**: Federal courts lack statutory authority under the Judiciary Act of 1789 to issue universal or nationwide injunctions that protect non-parties.20  
* **Section 706 APA Vacatur Survives**: Despite *CASA*'s limits on universal injunctions, federal courts retain statutory authority under Section 706 of the APA to vacate and set aside unlawful agency rules universally.10  
* **Procedural Due Process Requires Entitlement**: A due process claim requires proving a protected property or liberty interest created by an independent source, such as a statute or employment contract (*Trump v. Cook*).1  
* ***Mathews v. Eldridge*** **Calibrates Process**: What process is due before a state deprivation is determined by balancing the private interest, the risk of administrative error, and the government's fiscal burden.3  
* **Equal Protection Demands Purpose and Effect**: Establishing unconstitutional discrimination under the Fourteenth Amendment requires proving both a discriminatory effect and a discriminatory purpose.1  
* **RLUIPA Authorizes Individual Damages**: Congress used its Spending Clause and Necessary and Proper powers to condition federal funding on protecting inmate religious rights, authorizing individual-capacity damages suits (*Landor*).17  
* **Algorithmic Governance Requires Transparency**: Automated administrative systems must provide clear notice, plain-language explanations, and a meaningful right to human re-review to satisfy due process (*Colorado SB 26-189*).42  
* **Sovereign Immunity Excludes State-Created Corps**: State sovereign immunity does not protect state-created corporate entities that are fiscally independent and responsible for their own obligations (*Galette*).3  
* **Independent Agencies Face Removal Clash**: The constitutional boundary of Congress's authority to protect multi-member agency commissioners from at-will presidential removal is being directly re-evaluated (*Trump v. Slaughter*).8  
* **Qualified Immunity Shifts the Burden**: Once qualified immunity is asserted, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to prove the official violated a clearly established right under an identical factual precedent.52  
* **Emergency Powers Submit to Article I**: Even a declared national emergency does not authorize the executive branch to execute Congress's non-delegable constitutional power to impose taxes or tariffs.6  
* **Administrative Records Constrain Review**: Judicial review of agency action is restricted to the closed administrative record; extra-record evidence is inadmissible.1  
* **LLMs Require Strict Public-Law Discipline**: Automated legal systems must be aggressively constrained by finality, exhaustion, standing, sovereign immunity, and citator currentness to prevent hallucinated consensus.1

## **Annotated Source Architecture**

### **Constitutional Structure and Separation of Powers**

* *Vesting Clause of Article I, Section 1*: Vests all legislative powers in Congress, serving as the constitutional anchor for the nondelegation and major questions doctrines.6  
* *Humphrey's Executor v. United States* (1935): Landmark precedent holding that Congress may limit the President's authority to remove members of independent, multi-member agencies to instances of inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance.26  
* *Seila Law LLC v. CFPB* (2020): Limited *Humphrey's*, holding that Congress may not insulate a single-director independent agency wielding substantial executive power with for-cause removal protections.7  
* *Trump v. Slaughter* (pending 2026): A foundational case reviewing whether the statutory removal protections for FTC Commissioners violate the separation of powers and whether *Humphrey's Executor* should be overruled.8  
* *Trump v. Cook* (pending 2026): Reviewing whether a President can remove a Federal Reserve Governor for cause based on pre-appointment conduct without prior notice or a hearing, balancing executive supervision with central bank independence.9

### **Rights Enforcement and Civil Rights**

* *42 U.S.C. § 1983*: The primary statutory vehicle to enforce constitutional and federal civil rights against state and local officials acting under color of law.3  
* *The Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA)*: Enacted under the Spending Clause to protect the religious rights of incarcerated persons.17  
* *Tanzin v. Tanvir* (2020): Unanimously held that the Religious Freedom Restoration Act's provision for "appropriate relief" includes monetary damages against state officials in their individual capacities.17  
* *Landor v. Louisiana Department of Corrections* (pending 2026): Reviewing whether an individual may sue state prison employees in their individual capacities for damages under RLUIPA, resolving a critical remedy gap.17

### **Due Process and Equal Protection**

* *Mathews v. Eldridge* (1976): Established the three-factor balancing test to determine what procedural process is constitutionally due before a government deprivation.3  
* *Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill* (1985): Held that public employees who can only be terminated "for cause" possess a protected property interest, entitling them to a pre-termination hearing.1  
* *West Virginia v. B.P.J.* (pending 2026): Testing whether Title IX and the Equal Protection Clause prevent a state from categorically excluding transgender female students from athletic teams.15

### **Administrative Law and Judicial Deference**

* *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (2024): Overruled *Chevron*, requiring Article III courts to exercise independent judgment to find the best statutory meaning.1  
* *Skidmore v. Swift & Co.* (1944): The surviving standard of agency respect, assigning weight based on the thoroughness and consistency of the agency's reasoning.1  
* *West Virginia v. EPA* (2022): Formally established the Major Questions Doctrine, requiring clear congressional authorization for regulations of vast economic or political significance.1  
* *SEC v. Jarkesy* (2024): Held that the Seventh Amendment requires a jury trial in an Article III court, rather than an in-house ALJ tribunal, when an agency seeks civil penalties for claims akin to common-law fraud.1  
* *Corner Post, Inc. v. Board of Governors* (2024): Held that the APA’s six-year statute of limitations runs from the date the plaintiff is actually injured, not the date the regulation was finalized, exposing decades-old rules to fresh judicial review.1

### **Public-Law Remedies and Vacatur**

* *Trump v. CASA, Inc.* (2025): Landmark 6–3 decision holding that federal district courts lack equitable authority under the Judiciary Act of 1789 to issue universal or nationwide injunctions.20  
* *Administrative Procedure Act Section 706*: Instructs reviewing courts to "hold unlawful and set aside" arbitrary, capricious, or unlawful agency actions.10  
* *Fred Halbhuber, "A Remedy Inherited: State Law, Universal Vacatur, and the Meaning of 'Set Aside'"* (2025): Demonstrates that the APA's "set aside" language was borrowed from 19th-century state codes modeling judicial review on the appellate court-lower court relationship, which authorized universal vacatur.48  
* *American Hospital Association v. Kennedy* (D. Me. Dec 29, 2025): Post-*CASA* decision holding that *CASA* does not limit the APA’s traditional remedies, and that preliminary or permanent vacatur of an agency rule under Section 706 applies universally.10

### **Algorithmic Governance and Automated Decision-making**

* *Colorado Senate Bill 26-189* (passed May 12, 2026): Replaced the nation's first broad AI Act with a narrower notice- and disclosure-focused regime governing automated decision-making technology (ADMT), establishing a state-level public-law model.47
