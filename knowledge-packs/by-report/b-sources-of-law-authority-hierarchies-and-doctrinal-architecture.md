# B. Sources of Law, Authority Hierarchies, and Doctrinal Architecture

## **1. Executive Orientation**

This document serves as the authority-resolution layer of The Legal Mastery Canon. It inherits and operationalizes the theoretical operating system constructed in Report A, transitioning from the ontological question of what law is to the diagnostic mechanics of how law is ranked, applied, and defeated in active legal environments.1

Legal analysis frequently suffers from a systemic vulnerability: the conceptual collapse of distinct jurisdictional, procedural, and hierarchical dimensions into a flat landscape of semantic propositions. Untrained operators—and notably, large language models—tend to treat a quotation from a dissenting opinion, a trial court order, a superseded statute, and a binding constitutional provision as analytically equivalent simply because they share a semantic similarity to the issue at hand. This report prevents that collapse. It provides the rigorous structural architecture necessary to determine which legal propositions possess the institutional power to compel an outcome, which merely offer persuasive reasoning, which control procedural gateways without touching substantive rights, which are jurisdictionally displaced, and which are entirely devoid of legal force despite mimicking the aesthetic form of law.

## **2. Core Thesis**

The foundational premise governing this report is absolute: **legal sources are not merely containers of rules; they are institutionally ranked carriers of validity, authority, interpretive weight, procedural consequence, and doctrinal force.** **[binding legal proposition | well-established]**

The legal operator’s task is never simply to locate relevant text using semantic search. The task is to place each legal proposition precisely inside a multidimensional authority lattice defined by jurisdiction, forum, source type, issuing institution, hierarchy, chronology, procedural posture, precedential status, doctrinal role, and conflict position. The failure to map a legal proposition within this lattice strips the law of its institutional reality and reduces rigorous legal reasoning to mere associative word-matching. Semantic relevance is not legal authority; an argument's persuasive brilliance cannot overcome its lack of formal pedigree if a binding, contrary rule controls the forum.

## **3. Inherited Concepts from Report A**

To proceed effectively, this analysis inherits the conceptual primitives defined in Report A. Validity remains a strictly internal, formal determination of systemic pedigree, operating independently of external moral merit.1 Authority is understood as the systemic claim of a legal institution to generate content-independent, preemptive reasons for action, displacing the subject's first-order evaluations.1 Legitimacy functions as the normative or sociological justification of that power, while efficacy serves as the empirical measure of real-world compliance.1

Furthermore, this report integrates the layered model of law, tracking how abstract normative commands (first-order rights and duties) and second-order capacities (powers and liabilities) transition through the procedural and epistemic layers to culminate in coercive enforcement.1 The fundamental insight of legal pluralism—that state law operates alongside but distinctly from customary practice, market pressure, and private contractual ordering—is similarly presumed throughout the subsequent analysis.1

## **4. The Governing Question**

The cadence of all subsequent legal analysis is governed by a singular, relentlessly applied sequential inquiry:

**What controls? Where? When? Why? Against what? With what limits?**

A legal proposition floating free of these constraints is analytically useless. A legal rule never simply "is." It applies *somewhere* specific, binds *someone* specific, derives from *something* authorized, and yields to *higher authority* when challenged. To articulate a legal rule without immediately appending its jurisdictional anchor and hierarchical status is to state a fiction.

## **5. Source Taxonomy**

The legal system generates a vast ecology of texts, but only a fraction carry binding force. The following taxonomy distinguishes sources by their institutional pedigree, legal weight, and susceptibility to misuse. **[research method | well-established]**

| Source Category | Specific Instruments | Legal Force & Authority Basis | Verification & Potential Misuse |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Primary Authority** | Constitutions, enacted statutes, treaties, binding appellate precedent, municipal ordinances. | Carries mandatory, binding force within its specific jurisdiction. Derives authority directly from sovereign enactment or constitutional delegation.2 | **Verify:** Codification, effective dates, and negative treatment via citators.3 **Misuse:** Applying out-of-jurisdiction primary law as if it were binding. |
| **Delegated & Administrative Authority** | Agency regulations, administrative adjudications, executive orders. | Binds regulated parties and courts, provided the action remains strictly within the statutory delegation and complies with the Administrative Procedure Act (APA).4 | **Verify:** Statutory anchor and valid promulgation. **Misuse:** Treating a regulation struck down as *ultra vires* as operative law. |
| **Procedural Authority** | Rules of Civil/Criminal Procedure, local court rules, judge-specific standing orders. | Controls the mechanics of adjudication. Can trigger total dismissal without reaching substantive merits.5 | **Verify:** Live local docket rules. **Misuse:** Dismissing procedure as "mere technicality" when it is the gatekeeper of validity. |
| **Persuasive Authority** | Out-of-state precedent, unpublished opinions, intermediate appellate decisions from other circuits. | Influences judicial reasoning but cannot compel a result.2 Relied upon when mandatory authority is silent. | **Verify:** Jurisdictional origin and strict adherence to citation rules (e.g., FRAP 32.1).6 **Misuse:** Framing persuasive precedent as controlling. |
| **Secondary Authority** | Treatises, Restatements, model acts, law review articles, ALR annotations, encyclopedias. | Synthesizes and critiques primary law. Restatements may become quasi-binding if explicitly adopted by a high court.7 | **Verify:** Author reputation and date. **Misuse:** Citing a secondary source as if it independently binds a court. |
| **Soft Law & Agency Guidance** | Interpretive rules, policy statements, enforcement manuals, advisory letters, FAQ pages. | Lacks the force of law and cannot bind citizens, but heavily influences agency enforcement priorities and practical compliance.8 | **Verify:** Agency publication. **Misuse:** Mistaking a non-binding guidance document for a binding legislative regulation. |
| **Private Ordering Instruments** | Contracts, corporate bylaws, platform terms of service, arbitration agreements. | Binds specific parties contractually, creating practically sovereign ecosystems, but remains structurally subordinate to public state law.1 | **Verify:** Contract formation defenses and public policy limits. **Misuse:** Assuming a private rule overrides a mandatory public statute. |
| **Comparative & Historical Sources** | Foreign constitutional jurisprudence, ancient common law, customary legal materials. | Provides original public meaning, historical context, or comparative insight.1 Highly persuasive in novel systemic disputes. | **Verify:** Historical authenticity. **Misuse:** Applying historical custom that has been explicitly abrogated by modern statute. |

## **6. Authority Lattice**

The concept of a simple hierarchical "ladder" (Constitution > Statute > Regulation > Common Law Case) is fundamentally insufficient for modern legal practice. Authority operates as a multidimensional lattice. **[reasoned synthesis | well-established]** An authority's weight is strictly relative to the jurisdiction, the forum, the specific issue, the institutional competence of the issuer, the procedural posture, and chronological currentness.

The maxim "higher beats lower" is merely the beginning of the analysis. A constitutional provision undoubtedly trumps a conflicting statute. However, a specific statutory remedy will govern over a general constitutional directive if the statute was validly enacted to operationalize that constitutional right. A statute authorizes a regulation, and that regulation binds regulated parties with the force of law, but it binds a reviewing court only to the extent it does not exceed its enabling statute.9 A local court rule completely dictates filing deadlines and formatting, overriding any generalized substantive argument a litigant might make about their underlying rights.10 Agency guidance shapes an entire industry's behavior and triggers multi-million dollar compliance efforts, despite lacking the formal power to bind a federal judge.8 A dissent from a supreme court justice may become historically celebrated, yet it possesses zero controlling force in the case it accompanies. A Restatement of Torts remains a mere academic synthesis until a state supreme court explicitly adopts its specific section, at which point it instantaneously crystallizes into binding state law.7 To operate effectively, the legal practitioner must preserve these distinctions with absolute discipline.

## **7. Authority Hierarchy and Control Logic**

The control logic of legal systems balances vertical supremacy with horizontal boundaries and substantive imperatives against procedural gateways.

**Vertical Hierarchy:** Higher authorities displace lower ones. Federal law preempts conflicting state law under the Supremacy Clause; a state constitution invalidates conflicting state statutes; a state statute supersedes conflicting municipal ordinances.1 Within the judiciary, a supreme court decision binds all intermediate appellate and trial courts, while an intermediate appellate decision binds only the trial courts within its specific geographic circuit.2

**Horizontal Hierarchy:** Courts of equal rank do not bind one another. The Second Circuit Court of Appeals does not bind the Ninth Circuit. A federal district judge's opinion does not bind another district judge sitting in the exact same courthouse. When circuits split on the interpretation of a federal statute, the conflict remains a geographic reality until the Supreme Court intervenes to establish a uniform national rule.

**Substantive vs. Procedural Control:** The lattice strictly separates substantive rights from procedural enforcement.11 A party may possess an absolute, indisputable substantive right under a state property statute, yet forfeit that right entirely by violating a local procedural rule regarding the timing of a filing.10 Substantive authority defines the right; procedural authority dictates whether the forum will hear the claim.

**Comparative Awareness:** Common law systems view judicial precedent as binding (*stare decisis*), meaning a single appellate case can dictate future outcomes and generate new doctrinal rules.2 In stark contrast, civil law systems historically deny that a single judicial decision creates binding law, relying instead on comprehensive legislative codes. Civil law jurisdictions recognize past decisions only when they form a long, highly consistent chain of rulings known as *jurisprudence constante*, which carries persuasive weight but avoids the strict rigidity of *stare decisis*.12 Furthermore, in supranational regimes, domestic hierarchies are profoundly complicated; the European Union, for instance, asserts the primacy of EU law over the domestic constitutional law of its member states.14

## **8. Jurisdiction and Forum Discipline**

A legal proposition is never free-floating. It belongs somewhere. Before a rule can be articulated, the legal operator must identify the deciding forum and the governing jurisdiction. **[practical warning | well-established]**

When a lawsuit is filed in a United States federal court based on diversity of citizenship (parties from different states), the *Erie* doctrine dictates that the federal court must apply state substantive law to resolve the claim, ending the era of "general federal common law".5 However, under the corollary doctrine established in *Hanna v. Plumer*, the federal court applies federal procedural law—such as the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure—provided those rules are validly enacted under the Rules Enabling Act and do not abridge substantive rights.5

If the federal court must apply state substantive law under *Erie*, which specific state's law applies? The *Klaxon* doctrine commands that a federal district court must apply the choice-of-law rules of the state in which it physically sits.15 If a federal court sitting in Texas hears a tort case involving a crash in Virginia, it must use Texas's conflict-of-laws protocol to determine whether Texas or Virginia substantive law governs the dispute.16

Modern conflict of laws has largely shifted from rigid territorial rules (*lex loci delicti*, or the place of the wrong) to the Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws approach, famously pioneered in the New York case *Babcock v. Jackson*.17 Under this framework, the court determines which jurisdiction has the "most significant relationship" or the closest "grouping of contacts" to the occurrence and the parties, actively considering the underlying public policies of the competing states.19

Beyond the federal-state divide, forum discipline requires an awareness of arbitration regimes and tribal jurisdiction. If a dispute is bound by a valid arbitration clause, the procedural rules of the arbitral tribunal (e.g., AAA or JAMS) supersede public civil procedure, and substantive public law is applied by a private arbitrator whose decision is subject to highly restricted judicial review.

## **9. Precedent Architecture**

Judicial opinions are complex architectural structures containing distinct zones of authority. They are not uniform blocks of law. **[binding legal proposition | well-established]**

**Stare Decisis:** Vertical *stare decisis* absolutely compels lower courts to follow the decisions of higher courts within their direct chain of review.2 Horizontal *stare decisis* binds a court to its own prior decisions to ensure systemic predictability, though high courts retain the power to overrule themselves when doctrine proves unworkable. **Holding vs. Dicta:** The *ratio decidendi*—the rationale strictly necessary for the court to reach its judgment based on the specific material facts presented—constitutes the binding holding. *Obiter dicta*—statements of law unnecessary to the resolution of the case, hypothetical explorations, or broader philosophical observations—are persuasive at best and possess no binding force. **Opinion Anatomy:** A majority opinion commands the law. A plurality opinion resolves the specific case but fails to secure a majority for a single rationale, severely limiting its precedential weight. Concurrences agree with the ultimate judgment but offer alternative legal reasoning. Dissents disagree with both the judgment and the reasoning, serving as a historical record of objection but carrying zero immediate authority. **Publication Status:** The procedural rules governing publication dictate legal reality. Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure (FRAP) 32.1 permits lawyers to cite federal unpublished opinions issued on or after January 1, 2007, for persuasive value.6 Conversely, California Rule of Court 8.1115 strictly forbids the citation of unpublished California state appellate opinions, treating them as practically nonexistent for subsequent litigants, and threatens ethical sanctions for attorneys who violate this no-citation rule.22

## **10. Opinion Decomposition Method**

To prevent the extraction of dicta, dissenting views, or background summaries as controlling law, legal operators and AI architectures must decompose cases into structured objects. **[research method | well-established]**

1. **Court and Jurisdiction:** Identifies the precise position of the opinion in the vertical and horizontal hierarchy.  
2. **Date and Currentness:** Establishes chronology and flags subsequent negative treatment via citator algorithms.3  
3. **Procedural Posture:** Identifies how the case reached the court (e.g., motion to dismiss, summary judgment, post-trial appeal). This strictly determines the standard of review.  
4. **Material Facts:** Isolates the specific factual matrix that the court deemed legally relevant to ground its holding.  
5. **Issue Presented:** Formulates the exact legal question the court was asked to answer.  
6. **Governing Source:** Identifies the statute, regulation, or constitutional provision being interpreted or applied.  
7. **Holding:** Extracts the binding rule applied to the material facts to resolve the issue.  
8. **Reasoning:** Maps the logical steps, policy considerations, or historical analysis supporting the holding.  
9. **Disposition:** Notes the procedural outcome (e.g., Affirmed, Reversed, Remanded, Vacated).  
10. **Dicta/Concurrence/Dissent:** Segregates contextual language that provides philosophical insight or future pathways but possesses zero binding authority.

## **11. Statutory Architecture**

Statutes are meticulously structured legal machines, not continuous prose. Their architecture shapes their interpretation, and extracting rules without analyzing the surrounding structure invites critical failure. **[institutional practice | well-established]**

* **Definitions:** These provisions dictate the precise semantic boundaries of the text, frequently defining common words in highly unnatural, specialized ways (e.g., defining a corporation as a "person").24  
* **Scope Provisions:** Determine exactly who and what the machine regulates.  
* **Operative Provisions:** House the primary normative commands (the rights, duties, and prohibitions).24  
* **Exceptions and Safe Harbors:** Carve out immunities, neutralizing the operative provisions for specific actors or circumstances.  
* **Enforcement Provisions and Remedies:** Determine whether a private citizen can sue (a private right of action) or whether enforcement is exclusively delegated to the state.  
* **Severability Clauses:** Ensure that if a judicial body strikes down one provision of the statute as unconstitutional, the remainder of the statutory machine survives.24  
* **Savings Clauses:** Preserve preexisting common law rights or concurrent state regulations that the new statute might otherwise preempt.24  
* **Effective Dates and Sunsets:** Establish the temporal boundaries of validity.

To extract a single sentence from an operative provision while ignoring the definition section, the safe harbors, and the effective date is to amputate the law from its functional machinery.

## **12. Regulatory and Administrative Authority**

Administrative agencies project immense authority through rulemaking (legislative rules requiring notice-and-comment procedures) and adjudications (agency tribunals). However, agencies possess no inherent sovereign power; they derive their competence solely from enabling statutes passed by the legislature.1

The authority of these agencies has recently undergone a historic paradigm shift in United States federal law. **[recently changed | source-dependent]** For forty years, under the *Chevron* doctrine, federal courts deferred to an agency's reasonable interpretation of ambiguous statutory language, heavily tilting the balance of power toward the administrative state.25 In 2024, the Supreme Court explicitly overruled *Chevron* in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*.25 Under Section 706 of the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), courts must now exercise their independent judgment to find the single "best" meaning of a statute and will no longer defer to agencies simply because a statute is ambiguous.4

Consequently, agency interpretations of statutes are now relegated to *Skidmore* deference.4 Under *Skidmore*, an agency's interpretation receives "respect" or "weight" only proportional to its thoroughness, logical validity, and historical consistency—a persuasive weight rather than a binding one.8

Simultaneously, *SEC v. Jarkesy* severely limited agency adjudicatory authority, ruling that the Seventh Amendment requires a jury trial in an Article III federal court, rather than an in-house Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) tribunal, when an agency seeks civil penalties for claims akin to common-law fraud.9 Furthermore, *Corner Post* expanded the timeline to challenge regulations, holding that the APA's six-year statute of limitations runs from the date the plaintiff is actually injured by the agency action, not the date the regulation was finalized, exposing decades-old regulations to fresh judicial review.30

## **13. Common Law and Equity**

The common law is an inductive, judge-made doctrinal architecture that evolves incrementally through precedent.2 It remains valid and operative unless explicitly preempted or displaced by statutory enactments. **[historical explanation | well-established]**

Equity operates as a historically distinct system designed to provide flexible relief where rigid common law rules yield unjust results or inadequate compensation. Equity governs flexible remedial powers, including injunctions, specific performance, and the creation of trusts, operating under distinct historical doctrines such as *laches* (unreasonable delay), *unclean hands* (bad faith by the plaintiff), and *estoppel*. Equity is not synonymous with a modern judge's personal sense of fairness; it is a highly structured body of historical principles bounding judicial discretion. When modern statutes codify equitable remedies, they often incorporate this deep historical logic by implication, requiring the legal operator to look beyond the statutory text to the historical equitable tradition.

## **14. Court Rules, Local Rules, and Procedural Authority**

Procedural rules govern the epistemic and temporal boundaries of litigation. The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, local district rules, and judge-specific standing orders carry immense gatekeeping authority.5

Procedure is frequently dismissed by the laity as "mere technicality," but this is a fatal strategic error. A litigant possessing an ironclad substantive claim will routinely have their case dismissed with prejudice if they fail to observe a local procedural mandate regarding pleading standards, discovery deadlines, or service of process. As demonstrated in *Hanna v. Plumer*, valid federal procedural rules absolutely govern the mechanics of the forum, regardless of conflicting state laws.10 The procedural layer translates the abstract normative layer into coercive reality; if the procedural gates close, the substantive right effectively ceases to exist.1

## **15. Secondary and Persuasive Authority**

High-quality secondary sources—such as the Restatements of the Law, major multi-volume treatises (e.g., *Wright & Miller*), and peer-reviewed law review articles—serve to orient the researcher, synthesize fractured case law, and offer persuasive rationales for novel legal questions.19

However, secondary sources must never be cited as binding primary law. They are intellectual tools used to locate, compare, and explain primary authority. They achieve practical power only when a sovereign institution adopts them. For instance, the Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws—which established the "most significant relationship" test—was highly influential, but it became binding law only in the specific states where supreme courts explicitly adopted its provisions to replace older common law rules.7

## **17. Authority Lifecycle and Currentness Discipline**

Law is intensely temporal. A statute that was valid in 1990 may have been repealed in 2005. A Supreme Court precedent that anchored administrative law from 1984 (*Chevron*) was completely overruled in 2024 (*Loper Bright*).25 Before relying on any legal proposition, the researcher must validate its temporal lifecycle using an authoritative algorithmic citator (e.g., Shepard’s on Lexis, KeyCite on Westlaw, or BCite on Bloomberg Law).3

A **red flag** indicates the proposition is no longer good law (e.g., reversed, overruled, struck down, or repealed).33 A **yellow flag** indicates negative treatment that has not yet destroyed the precedent (e.g., criticized, limited to its facts, or distinguished by a later court).33 To cite a superseded statute or a reversed holding in a brief is a catastrophic failure of legal intelligence. The researcher must trace the exact line of subsequent treatment to ensure the specific point of law relied upon remains operative, as a case may be reversed on one ground but affirmed on the specific point the researcher intends to cite.33

## **18. Authority Resolution Protocol**

When sources collide, later reports and legal operators must execute the following protocol to resolve the conflict: **[research method | well-established]**

1. **Governing Jurisdiction:** Identify the sovereign territory governing the substantive dispute.  
2. **Deciding Forum:** Identify the court or tribunal. Apply *Erie* and *Klaxon* if a federal court is hearing a state-law claim.5  
3. **Issue Type:** Isolate whether the conflict is over a substantive right or a procedural mechanism.  
4. **Source Hierarchy:** Does a constitution conflict with a statute? (Constitution prevails). Does a statute conflict with a regulation? (Statute prevails).9  
5. **Institutional Competence:** Did the issuing body have the authority to act? Evaluate *ultra vires* action.  
6. **Delegation Problems:** Post-*Loper Bright*, does the agency regulation exceed the best reading of the enabling statute?.8  
7. **Chronology and Currentness:** Between two statutes of equal hierarchical rank, the later-in-time enactment generally prevails.  
8. **Specificity vs. Generality:** A specific statutory provision displaces a general provision addressing the same topic.  
9. **Procedural vs. Substantive Control:** Procedural rules control the mechanics of the forum; substantive rules control the underlying right.11  
10. **Constitutional Constraints:** Subject all lower rules to constitutional review.  
11. **Statutory Displacement:** Has a new statute explicitly displaced older common law doctrine?  
12. **Precedent Status:** Is the conflicting case binding vertical precedent, or non-binding horizontal/persuasive precedent?.2  
13. **Holding vs. Dicta:** Ensure the conflict involves actual *ratio decidendi*, not mere *obiter dicta*.  
14. **Factual and Doctrinal Similarity:** Can the conflicting cases be distinguished based on material facts?  
15. **Federalism and Supremacy:** Does valid federal law expressly or impliedly preempt state law?  
16. **Choice of Law:** If multiple jurisdictions are involved, apply the forum's conflict-of-laws protocol (e.g., Restatement Second's "most significant relationship" test).19  
17. **Administrative Deference:** Apply *Skidmore* respect based on the persuasiveness of the agency interpretation.8  
18. **Equitable Limits:** Does equitable doctrine (e.g., *laches*) block the enforcement of a legal right?  
19. **Unresolved Splits:** If a circuit split exists and the Supreme Court has not intervened, explicitly preserve the conflict. Identify the governing rule in the relevant circuit and do not synthesize a false national consensus.

## **19. Doctrinal Architecture Toolkit**

Legal doctrine is the structured synthesis of scattered legal materials into usable, operative propositions. A legal doctrine should be represented as a structured object containing:

* **Governing Source & Jurisdiction:** The anchor of the doctrine (e.g., state statute, federal precedent).  
* **Elements:** The mandatory components that a party must prove to establish a claim.  
* **Factors:** Considerations the court weighs (unlike elements, a single factor is rarely dispositive; courts use balancing tests).  
* **Exceptions/Defenses:** Safe harbors and affirmative defenses that neutralize the claim.  
* **Burdens and Presumptions:** Which party must produce evidence, and what legal fictions apply until rebutted.  
* **Standards of Proof:** The evidentiary threshold (e.g., preponderance of the evidence, clear and convincing, beyond a reasonable doubt).  
* **Standards of Review:** How an appellate court reviews the lower court (e.g., de novo, clear error, abuse of discretion).  
* **Currentness Status:** Verified citator history confirming the doctrine is still valid.3

## **20. Worked Examples**

**1. Statute Modified by Judicial Interpretation (Erie & Choice of Law):** A plaintiff sues in a federal district court in Texas for a personal injury sustained during a car crash in Virginia. The federal court applies federal procedural rules to govern the trial (*Hanna v. Plumer*).5 However, under the *Erie* doctrine, it cannot use federal common law; it must apply state substantive law.11 Under *Klaxon*, it applies Texas choice-of-law rules to determine which state's law governs.16 If Texas uses the Restatement (Second) "most significant relationship" test, the court weighs the contacts of both states and the underlying policies.19 If it concludes Virginia has the most significant relationship to the injury, the federal court in Texas applies Virginia tort law to decide the substantive merits of the case.34

**2. Agency Regulation Invalidated by Statute (Post-Chevron):** An environmental agency promulgates a regulation imposing severe industry fees, relying on an ambiguous enabling statute. A corporation challenges the rule. Previously, the reviewing court would have deferred to the agency under *Chevron*. Post-2024, under *Loper Bright*, the court exercises independent judgment to determine the best reading of the statute.8 It evaluates the agency's interpretation under *Skidmore* but finds it lacks historical consistency and logical thoroughness. The court strikes down the regulation as exceeding the statutory grant of authority.4 Furthermore, under *Jarkesy*, if the agency attempts to seek civil penalties for fraud-like conduct regarding the fees, it must do so in an Article III court with a jury, not in its own internal ALJ tribunals.9

**3. Circuit Split Preserved:**

The Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals rules that a specific police action violates the Fourth Amendment. The Eighth Circuit rules the identical action is constitutional. A legal researcher must not state "The law is unsettled," nor "The law generally permits..." The researcher must state: "In the Fourth Circuit, the action is unconstitutional; in the Eighth Circuit, it is permitted. The Supreme Court has not resolved the split." The controlling authority depends entirely on where the action occurs.

**4. Unpublished Opinion Local Rule Conflict:** An attorney practicing in a California state court finds a highly relevant, post-2007 unpublished California appellate decision. If they cite it in their brief to persuade the judge, they face severe ethical sanctions under California Rule of Court 8.1115.22 If the exact same attorney walks across the street into a federal district court and cites a post-2007 unpublished federal appellate decision, it is perfectly permissible and routinely accepted as persuasive authority under FRAP 32.1.6 The procedural venue dictates what counts as citable law.

**5. Agency Guidance Mistaken for Law:** A federal agency publishes an "FAQ" document on its website outlining aggressive new interpretations of a compliance statute. A compliance architect panics, treating the FAQ as binding law. A trained legal operator applies authority discipline: the FAQ is mere agency guidance. It did not go through notice-and-comment rulemaking, it lacks the force of law, and under *Loper Bright* and *Skidmore*, a court will merely give it "respect" if it is thoroughly reasoned, which informal FAQs rarely are.8 It signals enforcement priority, not binding legal reality.

## **21. Legal Pluralism and Non-State Normative Orders**

Building on Report A's recognition of legal pluralism 1, the operator must navigate ecosystems where state law interacts with non-state normative orders.

**Tribal Jurisdiction:** Under the landmark case *Montana v. United States*, Native American tribal courts generally lack civil regulatory jurisdiction over non-members acting on non-Indian fee land within the reservation boundaries.35 However, the Supreme Court established two vital exceptions where tribes retain inherent sovereign civil jurisdiction: (1) when non-members enter into consensual relationships with the tribe or its members (e.g., commercial dealings, leases), or (2) when non-member conduct threatens or has some direct effect on the political integrity, economic security, or health and welfare of the tribe.35

**Supranational Law:** In the European Union, the foundational case *Costa v ENEL* established the primacy of Community (EU) law over conflicting national laws of member states.14 It also affirmed the "direct effect" of certain treaty provisions, meaning individual citizens can invoke EU rights directly in their national courts against their own governments, fundamentally complicating traditional domestic constitutional hierarchies.39

**Private Ordering:** Mega-platforms and global corporations construct immense private governance systems through Terms of Service, Community Standards, and arbitration clauses.1 These systems mimic sovereign functions—establishing rules, employing automated algorithmic enforcement, and running private dispute resolution boards. They bind users contractually and exercise massive practical efficacy.1 However, they derive their authority from private contract law and remain structurally subordinate to the mandatory public law of the territorial sovereign.1

## **22. LLM Legal Research Discipline**

Large Language Models (LLMs) pose a profound, structural danger to legal reasoning because their transformer architecture naturally prioritizes semantic proximity over authority discipline. They synthesize plausible, law-like paragraphs by blending out-of-jurisdiction cases, superseded statutes, and dissenting opinions into a "hallucinated consensus." To prevent critical failures, legal AI systems and human users must enforce strict anti-hallucination heuristics: **[practical warning | well-established]**

* **Attach precise metadata:** Every generated legal proposition must be tagged with jurisdiction, source type, and authority status.  
* **Never substitute relevance for control:** Semantic relevance does not equal legal authority. A perfectly matched quote from a different circuit is useless if contrary binding precedent exists in the forum.  
* **Never average a split:** Unresolved jurisdictional splits must be explicitly preserved and geographically mapped, never blended into a fake consensus.  
* **Enforce Opinion Decomposition:** Prompt models to strictly isolate holdings from dicta, concurrences, and dissents before synthesizing rules.  
* **Verify publication status:** Treat unpublished decisions according to the specific procedural rules of the governing forum (e.g., FRAP 32.1 vs. CRC 8.1115).6  
* **Mandate Currentness Verification:** AI cannot be trusted to self-verify temporal validity; its outputs must be manually passed through a live citator (Shepard’s/KeyCite) to detect red or yellow flags.3  
* **Analyze Bindingness:** Never treat agency guidance or FAQs as equivalent to a binding legislative regulation.8

## **25. Key Takeaways**

| Principle | Strategic Implication for Legal Operators |
| :---- | :---- |
| **1. The Lattice over the Ladder** | Authority depends on jurisdiction, forum, institutional competence, and chronological status, not just a simple vertical hierarchy. |
| **2. Forum Dictates Reality** | The procedural rules of the specific deciding tribunal often dictate the survival of substantive rights (*Hanna v. Plumer*).10 |
| **3. Independent Judicial Review** | Post-*Loper Bright*, courts no longer blindly defer to agencies on ambiguous statutes, deploying independent judgment and assigning *Skidmore* weight instead.8 |
| **4. Substantive vs. Procedural Control** | Under *Erie*, federal diversity courts apply state substantive law and federal procedural law.11 |
| **5. Choice of Law Relativism** | Under *Klaxon*, a federal court applies the choice-of-law rules of the state in which it sits, often evaluating the "most significant relationship".16 |
| **6. The Holding Constraint** | Only the *ratio decidendi* binds; *obiter dicta* persuades. Decompose opinions meticulously. |
| **7. Publication Status Acts as Law** | The ability to cite an opinion depends entirely on local rules (e.g., CRC 8.1115 forbids California unpublished opinions; FRAP 32.1 permits federal ones).6 |
| **8. Statutes are Machines** | Definitions, exceptions, and severability clauses dictate the function of operative provisions.24 |
| **9. Currentness is Absolute** | Legal propositions are intensely temporal. A failure to verify negative treatment via citators (Shepard's/KeyCite) is fatal.33 |
| **10. Splits are Absolute** | Unresolved conflicts between jurisdictions must be geographically preserved and mapped, never averaged. |
| **11. Pluralism Boundaries** | Tribal jurisdiction over non-members depends strictly on consensual relations or political/economic threat (*Montana*).35 |
| **12. Supranational Primacy** | Regimes like the EU claim primacy and direct effect over national laws (*Costa v ENEL*).14 |
| **13. Agency Adjudication Limits** | Under *Jarkesy*, agencies cannot bypass Article III jury trials for fraud-like civil penalties.9 |
| **14. Secondary Sources Orient** | Treatises and Restatements map the law but possess zero binding force until explicitly adopted by a sovereign court.19 |
| **15. LLMs Require Structural Discipline** | AI models prioritize semantic matching. They must be aggressively constrained by jurisdiction, holding-isolation, and citator currentness to prevent hallucinated consensus. |

## **26. Annotated Source Architecture**

**Primary Law & Federalism Frameworks:**

* *Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins* (1938) & *Hanna v. Plumer* (1965): Establishes the substantive/procedural divide in federal diversity cases, ending general federal common law and affirming the supremacy of federal procedural rules.5  
* *Klaxon Co. v. Stentor Electric Mfg. Co.* (1941): Requires federal courts to apply the choice-of-law rules of the state in which they sit.16  
* *Babcock v. Jackson* (1963): Pioneers the "grouping of contacts" / "most significant relationship" test in conflicts of law, moving jurisprudence away from rigid vested territorial rights.17

**Administrative Law & Judicial Deference:**

* *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (2024): Overrules *Chevron*, requiring independent judicial interpretation under the APA.25  
* *Skidmore v. Swift & Co.* (1944): The surviving standard of agency respect, assigning weight based on the thoroughness and consistency of the agency's reasoning.4  
* *SEC v. Jarkesy* (2024): Limits agency in-house tribunals under the Seventh Amendment for civil penalties.29  
* *Corner Post, Inc. v. Board of Governors* (2024): Rules the APA statute of limitations runs from the moment of the plaintiff's injury, not the rule's finalization.30

**Procedural Rules & Publication:**

* *Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 32.1*: Permits citation of post-2006 unpublished federal opinions.6  
* *California Rules of Court, Rule 8.1115*: Forbids citation of unpublished state appellate opinions.23

**Comparative Law & Legal Pluralism:**

* *Costa v ENEL* (1964): Establishes EU law primacy and direct effect over member state law.14  
* *Montana v. United States* (1981): Defines the boundaries of tribal civil jurisdiction over non-members based on consensual relations and political integrity.36

**Legal Method and Citators:**

* *Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws*: Authoritative secondary source for modern choice-of-law methodology.19  
* *Shepard's Citations / Westlaw KeyCite*: The foundational algorithmic tools for mapping the currentness and lifecycle of legal authority, identifying superseded or overruled law.32

## **27. Open Questions for the Canon**

Future analytical modules must grapple with the following unresolved systemic tensions: **[emerging | uncertain]**

* As the post-*Loper Bright* landscape forces federal judges to exercise "independent judgment" on highly technical regulations, will courts achieve doctrinal stability, or will geographic circuit splits exponentially proliferate as different circuits find different "best" readings of ambiguous statutes? 29  
* How will the *Jarkesy* decision's restriction on agency tribunals reshape the enforcement architecture of modern administrative states, and will it paralyze agency efficiency by flooding Article III federal courts with complex regulatory litigation? 29  
* If digital platforms wield sovereign-like power but remain rooted in private contract, at what point does the traditional public/private law divide fail to protect citizens from systemic coercion, and will courts begin applying public-law constitutional norms to private digital infrastructure? 1  
* How will the legal profession ethically regulate the integration of LLMs in light of their fundamental architectural misalignment with jurisdictional discipline, holding extraction, and temporal validity?  
* With the rise of international arbitration and transnational private ordering (*lex mercatoria*), how long can traditional territorial choice-of-law frameworks (*Klaxon*, *Babcock*) adequately govern decentralized, cross-border digital disputes?
