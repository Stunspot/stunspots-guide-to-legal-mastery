# J. Private Law, Civil Obligation, and Liability Systems

## **I. Architectural Taxonomy of Private Law and Hohfeldian Relations**

The systemic architecture of private law functions as an autopoietic, operatively closed system of civil obligations, designed to translate raw social and commercial friction into a self-referential grammar of rights, duties, and liabilities.1 Where public law governs the vertical relationship between the sovereign state and the individual, private law establishes a horizontal, coordinate operating system that facilitates private ordering, protects individual interests, and allocates risk and loss.1 Under the rule of recognition, this horizontal system is cognitively open to historical and technological shifts but operatively closed, evaluating all external inputs exclusively through its own internal binary code of legal/illegal.1

To maintain conceptual rigor within this private-law responsibility framework, the legal operator must deploy the atomic legal grammar established by Wesley Newcomb Hohfeld.1 Hohfeldian legal relations prevent the analytical collapse of distinct entitlements, mapping first-order deontic relations (governing primary conduct) and second-order potestative relations (governing the alteration of primary relations).1 A critical insight of this framework is relational singleness, which dictates that every jural relation exists exclusively between exactly two persons.1 This relational singleness serves as the conceptual foundation for the doctrine of privity, restricting the enforcement of civil obligations to the immediate parties to a transaction or wrong.2

The horizontal operating system coordinates human and commercial behavior by separating first-order conduct rules from second-order remedial powers.1 A claim-right always requires a correlative duty of performance from a specific second party.1 In contrast, a liberty is a unilateral permission to act, correlated with a position of "no-right" in others; it imposes no duty of assistance or forbearance on the counterparty.1 The transition from these first-order conduct rules to second-order remedial liabilities occurs when a primary duty is breached.1 If a contracting party fails to perform, or a tortfeasor breaches a standard of care, the primary relational structure is transformed.1 The injured party’s first-order claim-right is extinguished or suspended, and they instantly acquire a second-order legal power to initiate a civil action.1 This power places the wrongdoer in a state of liability—meaning they are susceptible to having their financial assets or legal relations altered by a judicial judgment.1 To manage private-law risk, transactional engineers and litigators must preserve these boundaries, recognizing that liberties are not claim-rights, and immunities are not practical impunities.1

## **II. Tort Law, Modern Liability Systems, and Civil Justice Reforms**

### **Comparative Structures of Delictual Liability**

The allocation of civil losses resulting from non-contractual harms is governed by the law of torts in common-law jurisdictions and the law of delict in civil-law traditions.5 While these systems share the functional goals of deterrence, accident prevention, and victim compensation, their structural pathways diverge significantly.5 Civil-law systems are built upon broad, general principles of fault-based liability codified in unified instruments.5 This civilian approach derives from the Roman *Lex Aquilia*, establishing a singular, integrated concept of extra-contractual responsibility. For example, Article 1382 of the French Civil Code (and its domestic derivative, Louisiana Civil Code Article 2315) states that any act of man that causes damage to another obliges him by whose fault it happened to repair it.5 Under this model, the court applies a deductive reasoning process, matching the facts of a dispute to the open-textured statutory elements of harm, conduct, causation, and fault. Scots and Roman-Dutch law retain this delictual structure, dividing delictual remedies into two primary actions: the *actio legis Aquiliae* for patrimonial or economic loss, and the *actio iniuriarum* for non-patrimonial or injuries to real rights and dignitary interests.

Conversely, the common law of torts was historically structured as a decentralized, inductive network of distinct, independent causes of action, such as trespass, conversion, nuisance, and defamation. No single, unifying principle of liability existed until the twentieth-century development of the general duty of care in negligence.5 Even in the modern era, common-law courts continue to develop tort doctrine incrementally through case-by-case analogical reasoning on a closed record, rather than deducing liability from a comprehensive legislative text.1

### **The Restatement Third of Torts: Evolution of Liability Rules**

The American Law Institute’s ongoing drafting of the *Restatement (Third) of Torts* represents a significant, structured shift in the rules of private responsibility. This evolution is marked by several distinct modules designed to modernize classical tort principles:

The 2010 *Restatement (Third) of Torts: Liability for Physical and Emotional Harm* reversed decades of accepted doctrine by abolishing the traditional common-law categories of licensees, invitees, and trespassers in favor of a generalized duty of reasonable care owed to all land possessors, subject to a narrow exception for flagrant trespassers.8 This shift drew intense pushback from corporate and defense interests, led by the American Tort Reform Association, which successfully lobbied numerous state legislatures to codify the traditional, highly restrictive common-law categories in statute, freezing the law in place to prevent judicial adoption of the Restatement standard.8

The *Restatement of the Law Third, Torts: Liability for Economic Harm* (2020) addresses the boundaries between contract and tort, establishing rules for the unintentional infliction of economic loss.9 It codifies the economic-loss rule under Section 3, precluding tort liability for pure economic loss when the parties' relationship is governed by a contract, thereby forcing parties to allocate economic risk through private ordering.11 It also defines the narrow, non-contractual exceptions where tort liability attaches to professional negligence, negligent misrepresentation, negligent performance of services, and public nuisance resulting in economic loss.9

Under the pending *Concluding Provisions*, the draft restatements seek to codify a controversial medical monitoring rule.8 This rule would permit a plaintiff exposed to toxic substances to recover the costs of ongoing diagnostic testing in the absence of a present, clinically manifest physical injury.8 This draft has drawn sharp criticism for bypassing the traditional physical-injury requirement of tort law, conflicting with several state courts that restrict medical monitoring claims to prevent speculative recoveries by the unimpaired.8

The newly approved *Restatement (Third) of Torts: Remedies* codifies the foundational "rightful position" standard in Section 2, declaring that a plaintiff who establishes liability in tort is generally entitled to a remedy that will place that plaintiff, as nearly as possible, in the position they would have occupied if the tort had not occurred.[15, r2, r5] Section 4 of this Restatement introduces a major analytical simplification, declaring that traditional distinctions between immediate and consequential, direct and consequential, or general and special damages are ill-defined and do not affect the availability or measurement of tort damages, thereby shifting focus to direct causation and foreseeability.[15, r2, r5] Furthermore, this Restatement reorganizes and expands the treatment of market-immeasurable damages, including dignitary harms, emotional distress, constitutional harms, and novel injuries such as the loss of the human-pet bond after intentional torts.[102, s1, s2]

### **Civil Justice Reforms and State Tort Law Trends**

During 2025 and 2026, corporate defendants and insurance systems have focused heavily on combatting "nuclear verdicts" and escalating tort awards through coordinated state civil justice reforms.14 This active legislative period has produced distinct regional patterns of reform, altering the balance of power between the plaintiffs' bar and corporate defendants:

* **Damage Caps and Constitutional Challenges:** Some states have actively sought to limit noneconomic damages.14 Montana enacted legislation in 2025 doubling the state's cap on noneconomic damages in medical liability cases by the end of the decade, representing a compromise to preserve the cap against constitutional challenges.14 Meanwhile, other states like Georgia, Oklahoma, and Arkansas addressed tort awards through statutory limits.14 However, these caps face severe constitutional headwinds; in 2025, two Ohio appellate courts declared a statutory limit on noneconomic damages in medical liability cases unconstitutional as applied to child victims of intentional criminal conduct under the state constitution's "due course of law" clause, setting up a decisive review by the Ohio Supreme Court in 2026.[r2, r5, 32]  
* **Third-Party Litigation Funding (TPLF) and Consumer Lending:** The regulation of third-party litigation funding emerged as a major trend in 2025.14 Arizona, Colorado, Georgia, Kansas, Montana, and Oklahoma enacted statutes regulating commercial TPLF, joining Indiana, Louisiana, West Virginia, and Wisconsin in requiring disclosures of funding agreements to prevent asymmetric settlement leverage.14 Simultaneously, New York passed legislation regulating consumer lawsuit lending to protect vulnerable plaintiffs from predatory interest rates.14  
* **Pesticide Labeling and Regulatory Preemption:** North Dakota and Georgia passed laws in 2025 establishing that agricultural pesticides carrying an EPA-approved label are legally sufficient to satisfy any state-law duty to warn or label, effectively preempting failure-to-warn tort claims and insulating manufacturers from massive design-defect exposure.14  
* **Wrongful Death Statutes and Temporal Limits:** In 2025, Louisiana extended its prescriptive period (statute of limitations) for wrongful death and survival actions, expanding the temporal window for plaintiffs to bring claims.14 Conversely, pro-plaintiff expansions of wrongful death damages stalled in other major states; Florida Governor Ron DeSantis vetoed legislation that would have expanded the scope of damages under Florida's Wrongful Death Act, while New York Governor Kathy Hochul, for a fourth time, vetoed legislation that would have significantly expanded the classes of beneficiaries and damages available in New York wrongful death actions.14

### **Technology-Induced Liability Shifts: Algorithmic Defect and Conversational AI**

The rapid integration of consumer-facing artificial intelligence and engagement-maximizing social media algorithms has placed classical product liability law under unprecedented pressure.[40, s1, s2] Historically, product liability was confined to tangible personal property; software was treated as a service, requiring plaintiffs to satisfy the heightened burden of proving negligence. However, converging litigation tracks are actively dismantling this tangibility-based dichotomy.[53, r21]

In a series of landmark rulings, including the social media multidistrict litigation and the conversational AI chatbot proceedings, courts have begun applying a functionality-based test for product status.[53, s13] In *Garcia v. Character Technologies, Inc.* (785 F. Supp. 3d 1157, M.D. Fla. May 20, 2025), a wrongful death action arising from the suicide of a minor who formed an intense emotional relationship with an anthropomorphic AI chatbot, the court denied the defendants' motion to dismiss, holding that the chatbot app functioned as a "product" for the purposes of design defect and failure-to-warn claims.[15, r21, s13] The court carefully localized the defect, distinguishing between the "service" aspect of dynamically generated ideas or expressions (which may be protected speech or exempt under Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act) and the "product" aspect of the app's interactive interface, such as the absence of age-verification, the lack of parent-control guardrails, and friction-inducing prompts.[40, s13, s28]

This litigation wave raises profound questions under the *Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability*:

The application of the "reasonable alternative design" (RAD) test to adaptive, emergent software systems is conceptually challenging.[53, s28] Plaintiffs must plead and prove a technologically and economically feasible alternative design.[s13, s28] In the context of generative AI, defendants argue that a "safer" algorithmic design—such as implementing highly restrictive guardrails—merely results in a less functional, less effective product, which may fail the risk-utility balancing test.[53, r21, s28]

The *Garcia* court went significantly further than prior social media rulings by holding that Google could be held plausibly liable as a "component part manufacturer".15 The plaintiff alleged that Google built and made publicly available the underlying Large Language Model (LLM) and provided the technical cloud infrastructure to power the chatbot, and that the LLM itself was the defective component that caused the downstream app's harmful behavior.15 This establishes a major precedent exposing upstream foundational model developers to downstream liability, a theory also explored in *Matthew and Maria Raine v. OpenAI* (filed August 2025) involving similar allegations of chatbot-encouraged self-harm.16

AI chatbot developers are finding it increasingly difficult to assert Section 230 immunity.15 Section 230 protects interactive computer services acting in the traditional role of a publisher or curator of third-party, user-generated content.15 However, where the AI chatbot dynamically generates its own highly personalized, conversational outputs, it acts as an "information content provider" in its own right, stripping it of statutory immunity.15 Furthermore, courts have declined to dismiss these claims on First Amendment grounds, refusing to shield commercially deployed, anthropomorphic conversational engines from traditional tort duties of care.[40, s29]

Because software products are continuously updated, courts are increasingly willing to impose an ongoing post-sale duty to warn and implement safety features as evidence of harm accumulates.17

## **III. Property Law, Nuisance, and Subsurface Governance**

### **Subsurface Trespass and Geologic Carbon Sequestration**

The transition toward carbon capture, utilization, and geologic carbon storage (CCUS) has forced property law to confront the spatial boundaries of ownership.[28, r22, r28] To achieve net-zero objectives, developers are proposing to inject carbon dioxide and wastewater into deep subsurface basin-scale geological formations, known as "pore space".[28, r22, r28] This growing industrial demand has triggered a profound jurisprudential debate over the nature of subsurface trespass, reflected in the drafting of the *Restatement (Fourth) of Property*.[r22]

The Fourth Tentative Draft of the *Restatement (Fourth) of Property*, building on the essentialist property theories of Henry Smith and Thomas Merrill, prescribes treating all physical entries below ground as ordinary trespasses.[r22] This includes shallow subsurface intrusions (foundations, utility lines) as well as deep subsurface invasions (hydraulic fracturing fluids, wastewater disposal, carbon dioxide plumes).[r22] Under this "essentialist" model, the law presumes harm to the landowner from the mere fact of physical entry into their vertical column of ownership, extending the classical Latin maxim *cuius est solum, eius est usque ad coelum et ad inferos* (whoever owns the soil, owns up to the heavens and down to the depths).[r22]

Property and energy law scholar Joseph Schremmer has delivered a rigorous critique of the Restatement's approach, demonstrating that the case law does not support applying ordinary, per se trespass to deep subsurface fluid invasions.[r22] Schremmer proposes a modified "harm-based" account of subsurface trespass [r22]:

Pore space and common reservoirs are fluid, interconnected resources that are incapable of exclusive physical control.[r22] Applying the standard trespass model—where harm is presumed from the mere fact of entry—is an awkward fit for resources that naturally migrate.[r22]

Drawing on established oil and gas jurisprudence, Schremmer argues that property rights in shared reservoirs are correlative and nonexclusive.[r22] Therefore, a subsurface invasion by an injected fluid (such as carbon dioxide or wastewater) should only constitute an actionable trespass if the plaintiff can prove actual, material harm—such as physical damage to water wells, active mineral deposits, or the destruction of commercial development potential.[r22]

To facilitate geologic sequestration, states are actively modifying their property codes to open up pore space.[r28, r39] For example, West Virginia’s SB 267 (2025) opened pore space to commercial carbon storage, demonstrating how legislatures are establishing a statutory baseline to override the restrictive common-law veto power of surface owners.[r39]

In state tort development, the Court of Appeals of Utah in *Jeremy Rosser and Brandon Kilburn v. LLC JPC* (April 2026) addressed a similar boundary, holding that while a claim of negligent trespass is not recognized in Utah, adjacent landowners can successfully pursue claims for negligence, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and private nuisance for subsurface and surface disruptions, reinforcing the shift toward harm-based evaluations of property interference.

### **The Restatement Fourth of Property and the Locality Rule in Nuisance**

Nuisance law is the primary common-law mechanism regulating conflicting land uses.[s8] Under the *First and Second Restatements of Torts*, private nuisance determinations were highly unpredictable, requiring courts to engage in an ad hoc, multi-factor utility balancing test.[s8] A court would weigh the gravity of the harm to the plaintiff (such as noise, vibration, or odor) against the social utility of the defendant's conduct, and evaluate the "suitability" of the activity to the character of the locality.[s8] This approach often led to inconsistent results, creating severe property-rights uncertainty and encouraging opportunistic behavior by land developers or vexatious neighbors.[s8]

The upcoming *Restatement (Fourth) of Property* attempts to resolve this indeterminacy by elevating the "locality rule" to the primary determinant of reasonableness.[s8] Under the current draft, a defendant's land-use activity is presumptively reasonable and non-abusive if it is consistent with the established, predominant activities in the surrounding geographic area.[s8] While this shift reduces judicial discretion and lowers transaction and information costs, property theorists warn that it carries severe urbanizing distortions [s8]:

By defining reasonableness strictly by the existing local baseline, the locality rule can function as a rigid, judicially managed zoning tool that locks in the status quo.[s8]

In transitioning or gentrifying neighborhoods, the locality rule tends to discourage positive economic change.[s8] A new, socially beneficial business or residential development that introduces novel sounds or activities may be vulnerable to nuisance injunctions simply because it departs from the historical, potentially decaying character of the industrial or rural locality.[s8] This highlights the ongoing battle between progressive theorists who view nuisance as flexible ad hoc zoning and essentialist theorists who seek rigid, predictable rules.[r88, s8]

### **Donative Succession: Capacity and Coercion in Private Wealth Transfer**

The transfer of private wealth at death is governed by the *Restatement (Third) of Property: Wills and Other Donative Transfers*, which establishes the legal baseline for evaluating donative intent against challenges of incapacity or coercion [r4, s7]:

* **Testamentary, Donative, and Contractual Capacity:** Private law establishes distinct mental capacity scales depending on the nature of the transfer.[r10, r89] While testamentary capacity requires only a modest understanding of the property and the heirs at the time of execution, donative capacity (required for lifetime, irrevocable gifts) is held to a significantly higher standard.[r10, r89] Under Section 8.1, because an irrevocable gift depletes financial resources that the donor may yet need, they must be capable of understanding not only the transaction but also the direct effect the gift will have on their future financial security and dependents.[r89] Both standard testamentary and donative capacity are narrower than general mental or contractual capacity; a mildly impaired individual may lack the capacity to manage complex business transactions but still retain the autonomy to execute a valid will.[r10]  
* **The Goodfellow Standard:** To establish valid testamentary capacity, the state courts rely on the classic *Goodfellow* standard.[r10] The testator must simultaneously (1) understand the nature and purpose of the act of making a will, (2) know the nature and extent of their property, (3) recognize the natural objects of their bounty (close family members and dependents), and (4) be capable of relating these elements in a coherent, rational plan of distribution.[r10]  
* **Undue Influence and the Abolition Debate:** To set aside a donative transfer for coercion, a challenger carries the burden to prove undue influence under Section 8.3.[r89] This requires demonstrating that another person exercised such dominant and controlling influence over the donor's mind that the transfer ceased to act of their free volition and instead reflected the wielder’s intent.[r89, s61]  
  This doctrine has drawn sharp scholarly criticism.[r37] Property scholar Carla Spivack has delivered a comprehensive critique calling for the outright abolition of the undue influence doctrine, demonstrating that its historical migration from equity to common law distorted its application.[r37] Spivack argues that the doctrine fails to meet baseline standards of clarity, fairness, or predictability, and relies on outdated psychological models of autonomy.[r37] In practice, the doctrine is frequently weaponized by biological families to strike down non-traditional bequests to unmarried partners, friends, or caregivers, functioning as a tool of social conformity rather than elder protection.[r37]

### **Family Property Allocations: Community Property vs. Equitable Distribution**

Marital property division represents a major domain of private-law asset allocation, split across two distinct legal systems [r103, r111]:

* **Community Property States:** A minority of nine states (including California and Texas) operate under a community property system.[r103, r115] Under this partnership model, all assets and wages acquired by either spouse during the marriage are deemed community property and divided exactly 50/50 upon divorce, regardless of misconduct, economic disparities, or the duration of the union.[r103, r107, r114] Separate property is limited to assets acquired prior to marriage or through individual gifts and inheritances.[r103, r114] This system provides maximum predictability and rapid administrative resolution, bypassing complex judicial valuations.[r103, r115]  
* **Equitable Distribution States:** The majority of forty states apply the rules of equitable division.[r103, r115] Rather than splitting assets equally, courts are granted massive judicial discretion to divide marital property "fairly" based on the unique circumstances of each spouse.[r103, r107, r111] Judges evaluate factors such as individual earning capacities, age, health, future financial needs, and direct or indirect contributions to the marriage (including childcare and homemaking).[r103, r111, r114] While this flexibility allows for highly tailored, equitable outcomes, it also creates significant unpredictability, prolonging litigation and increasing transaction costs.[r111, r114]

## **IV. The Fiduciary Domain, Entity Governance, and Contractual Restraints**

### **Fiduciary Duties in Trust Administration: Loyalty, Prudence, and ESG Governance**

Fiduciary relationships represent a specialized class of civil obligations characterized by an extreme asymmetry of trust and control, requiring the dominant party (the fiduciary) to satisfy strict, non-market standards of care and loyalty.[r43, r50] Under the *Restatement (Third) of Trusts*, these duties are formulated with absolute rigor:

* **The Duty of Loyalty and the "Sole Interest" Rule:** Trust law frames the duty of loyalty with unmatched stringency, implementing the "sole interest" rule.[r43, r27] Section 78 of the Restatement dictates that the trustee is under a strict, non-waivable duty to administer the trust solely in the interest of the beneficiaries.[r43, r52] Except in highly restricted circumstances, a trustee is strictly prohibited from engaging in any transaction involving self-dealing or creating a conflict between their fiduciary duty and personal interest, regardless of whether the transaction was executed in good faith or was commercially fair.[r43, r52]  
* **The Duty of Prudence and the Prudent Investor Rule:** The duty of care, or prudence, is an objective standard requiring the trustee to administer the trust as a prudent person would, exercising reasonable care, skill, and caution in light of the trust's purposes, terms, and distribution requirements.[r43, r52] Crucially, unlike corporate law, the fiduciary duty of care in trust administration is not softened by a business judgment rule.[r43] Under the Prudent Investor Rule (Restatement (Third) of Trusts Section 227), trustees must manage assets holistically, balancing risk and return, and are ordinarily required to diversify investments as a fundamental risk-management discipline.[r68, r80]  
* **The ESG and Social Conscience Clash:** The rise of environmental, social, and governance (ESG) investing has created a profound fiduciary conflict.[r27, r80] Trust law scholars, drawing on the Restatement, decompose ESG into two distinct categories [r27]:  
  1. *Risk-Return ESG:* The trustee utilizes ESG factors purely as material risk indicators to exploit market mispricing and maximize financial returns for the beneficiaries.[r27] This is legally permissible under the duty of prudence, provided the trustee maintains a documented, reasonable financial analysis.[r27]  
  2. *Collateral Benefits ESG:* The trustee selects investments for moral, ethical, or political reasons, or to secure benefits for third parties or the environment.[r27, r80] This constitutes a direct violation of the sole interest rule and the duty of loyalty, unless explicitly authorized by the trust instrument or the unanimous consent of the beneficiaries.[r27, r80] This sole-interest ideology has served as the primary model for state-level anti-ESG pension regulations, such as Indiana's anti-ESG law, which forbids public pension trustees from sacrificing financial return for collateral social goals.[r57]

### **ERISA Fiduciaries and the "Should Have Known" Standard in the Era of AI**

The intersection of technology and ERISA fiduciary duties has created a new landscape of liability.[r25] In the retirement plan context, courts and regulators look directly to the *Restatement (Third) of Trusts* for interpretive guidance, following the Supreme Court’s mandate in *Tibble v. Edison International* to apply common-law trust principles to ERISA monitoring obligations.[r25, r50]

Under Section 78(3) and 79(3) of the Restatement, fiduciaries face severe exposure under the "knew or should have known" liability standard.[r25] The duty to monitor is an ongoing obligation, requiring fiduciaries to conduct regular, proactive reviews of investment options, fee structures, and service providers.[r25, r50] The "should have known" standard is objective, evolving alongside the technological tools and analytical capabilities available in the fiduciary community.[r25]

The rapid proliferation of AI-based research, predictive analytics, and automated monitoring systems has raised the legal baseline of reasonable prudence.[r25] If a retirement plan sponsor or investment committee fails to utilize—or at least consider—readily available AI tools for fee benchmarking, ESG risk screening, and market anomaly detection, they may be deemed to have constructive knowledge of risks or systemic overcharges that a standard AI audit would have instantaneously revealed.[r25] Autonomy and a "pure heart" are legally irrelevant; fiduciaries who manage trillions of dollars of other people's money cannot escape liability through a "pure heart and an empty head".[r25, r50]

### **Fiduciary Waivers in Delaware LLCs and the Implied Covenant of Good Faith**

In corporate and entity law, there exists a fundamental division between the rigid, structural fiduciary duties of corporations and the highly flexible, contractarian framework of limited liability companies.[r44, r51] Under Delaware law, corporate directors cannot contractually eliminate their core fiduciary duties of loyalty and care.[r44, r51] However, Section 18-1101(c) of the Delaware Limited Liability Company Act grants parties complete freedom of contract, permitting them to expand, restrict, or entirely eliminate all fiduciary duties in their operating agreement.[r26, r51]

The Delaware Supreme Court’s summary affirmance of *Faiz Khan and Ralph Finger v. Warburg Pincus, LLC, et al.* (November 2025) represents a landmark victory for private equity and fund sponsors, establishing a definitive "owner's manual" for executing fiduciary duty waivers.[r42, r44, r51] In *Khan*, the minority physician-members of urgent care provider CityMD challenged a merger with VillageMD negotiated by majority owner Warburg Pincus.[r14, r42, r51] The minority members received a mix of cash and VillageMD equity (which subsequently declined in value), while the Warburg investors negotiated a senior, disparate cash payout of $3.3 billion.[r14, r42, r51] Because the merger required amending the LLC agreement to eliminate the minority’s "tag-along" rights, the plaintiffs sued post-closing, alleging that Warburg coerced the minority class and breached the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.[r14, r42, r51]

The Delaware Court of Chancery dismissed all claims with prejudice, and the Supreme Court affirmed, reinforcing several key contractarian principles [r44, r51, r84]:

* **Enforceability of Blanket Waivers:** Because the CityMD LLC agreement disclaimed all fiduciary duties and explicitly authorized Warburg to act "exclusively in its own interest," Warburg owed no duty of loyalty or fairness to the minority when structuring the disparate merger consideration.[r14, r26, r42]  
* **The Implied Covenant is Not a Fiduciary Substitute:** The Court of Chancery fiercely rejected the plaintiffs' attempt to repackage fairness, disclosure, or coercion claims as breaches of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.[r44, r51, s21] The implied covenant cannot be bent into a backdoor "fiduciary substitute".[r44, r51, s21] It is a narrow gap-filling doctrine that applies only when a contract is truly silent on the dispute.[r26, r51, s21] Because the CityMD LLC agreement explicitly addressed amendment mechanics, class-consent voting, and minority tag-along rights, there was no contractual "gap" for the court to fill.[r26, r51, s21]  
* **No Free-Floating Duty of Disclosure:** In a Delaware corporation, directors owe a fiduciary duty of full material disclosure when seeking stockholder votes.[r44] In an LLC that has waived fiduciary duties, however, there is no free-floating duty of disclosure; the scope of required disclosure is governed strictly by the contract's notice provisions (which, in *Khan*, required only "reasonable and sufficient notice," not full disclosure).[r26, r30, r51]  
* **Jurisdictional Fragmentation (Delaware vs. California):** Transactional designers must recognize that Delaware’s contractarian permissiveness is not universal.[r26, r42] Under the Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act (RULLCA), California courts may refuse to enforce Delaware-style blanket waivers of fiduciary duties if they find evidence of fraud, active oppression, or if the LLC has substantial California connections, highlighting the importance of choice-of-law and forum-selection discipline.[r26]

## **V. Unjust Enrichment, Quasi-Contract, and Restitutionary Frameworks**

### **Substantive Autonomy of Unjust Enrichment**

The publication of the *Restatement (Third) of Restitution and Unjust Enrichment* (R3RUE) permanently established unjust enrichment as a standalone, third branch of the common law of obligations, standing alongside contract and tort.[r1, r48] The core organizing thesis of the R3RUE was to pry the historical common-law action of quasi-contract and the equitable remedy of constructive trusts away from their false homes in contract and trust law, unifying them into a single, coherent doctrinal framework based on the prevention of unjustified enrichment.[r48, r74]

Under Section 1 of the R3RUE, a liability in unjust enrichment requires proving two fundamental elements: (1) the receipt of a benefit by the defendant, and (2) the unjust retention of that benefit at the expense of another.[r29, r45] Under Comment d, the benefit may take any form, direct or indirect, including a savings of expense or the acquisition of an opportunity.[r29, r45] Enrichment is "unjust" or "unjustified" when it lacks an adequate legal basis, meaning it is not supported by a valid contract, statutory authority, or donative intent.[r29, r45]

Historically, unjust enrichment arose at common law, but its modern application spans both law and equity.[r67] Pleading a standalone claim for unjust enrichment in a court of equity typically requires demonstrating the absence of an adequate remedy at law, a procedural gate strictly enforced in states such as Florida, Iowa, Kansas, and Louisiana.[r55, r67] If a plaintiff possesses a viable contract claim for the same injury, the unjust enrichment claim must be dismissed.[r51, r55] However, in Delaware, if the court has equity jurisdiction over a primary claim (such as a breach of fiduciary duty), it can exercise jurisdiction over the related unjust enrichment claim under the "clean-up doctrine," even if an adequate remedy at law exists.[r67]

### **Procedural Gates and Pleading Restrictions**

The pleading of quasi-contractual and restitutionary claims is subject to highly restrictive jurisdictional rules.[r55] In multidistrict litigation (MDL) proceedings, such as *In re Valsartan* (MDL 2875), courts apply local state pleading rules to screen out unjust enrichment claims [r55]:

* **Adequate Remedy at Law Gate:** Florida, Iowa, Kansas, and Louisiana require plaintiffs to affirmatively plead the absence of an adequate remedy at law to advance an unjust enrichment claim.[r55]  
* **Bilateral Contract Bars:** Alabama, Florida, Hawaii, Idaho, Illinois, Louisiana, Massachusetts, Mississippi, Oklahoma, South Carolina, and West Virginia prohibit plaintiffs from pleading unjust enrichment if an express, enforceable contract covers the same subject matter, requiring dismissal of the quasi-contractual count at the Rule 12(b)(6) stage.[r55, r51]  
* **Performance of Illegal Agreements:** Section 32 of the R3RUE addresses transactions where a party renders performance under an agreement that is illegal or unenforceable for reasons of public policy.[r6] Restitution of the benefit conferred is allowed if required by the policy of the underlying prohibition, or as necessary to prevent unjust enrichment.[r6] However, under Section 32, there is no unjust enrichment if the claimant has received the exact counterperformance specified in the agreement, even if the contract itself was technically void.[r6]  
* **Three-Party Restitutionary Restraints:** In "three-party" scenarios—where a third party transfers value to the defendant that should have gone to the plaintiff—restitution is restricted.[r60] In cases of simple mistaken payment (such as a bank paying the wrong recipient), neither the payor nor the recipient has engaged in wrongful conduct.[r60] The intended recipient may pursue restitution only if they can prove a superior equitable claim to the specific funds, preventing windfalls while preserving transactional finality.[r60]

### **Restitution for Wrongs vs. Compensatory Damages**

Private law remedies are characterized by a fundamental distinction between compensation-based recovery and gains-based recovery.[r45, r71] Compensatory damages measure what the plaintiff has lost as a result of the defendant’s breach of duty, seeking to place the victim in their "rightful position".[r2, r5, r71] In contrast, restitution and disgorgement focus entirely on the defendant, measuring what the defendant has gained as a result of their wrongful act, and forcing them to give up those profits to the plaintiff.[r45, r63, r71]

This gains-based remedy, termed **Restitution for Wrongs**, is available in circumscribed situations where compensation alone would fail to provide adequate deterrence [r63, r64, r71]:

* **Fiduciary Conflicted Transactions:** Fiduciaries are ordered to disgorge profits earned from conflicted transactions or bribes without any inquiry into whether the beneficiary was harmed.[r7] This principle, dating back to *Keech v. Sandford* (1726) and reinforced by the Supreme Court in *Jackson v. Smith* (1921), holds that a fiduciary must account for all profits even if the trust estate was not injured by the transaction, as the rule of undivided loyalty must be strictly pursued.[r7]  
* **Intellectual Property Infringement:** An infringer who reaches new customers or transforms a copyrighted work or reaches new customers may generate profits without diverting a single sale from the plaintiff.[r7] The plaintiff can nonetheless recover the defendant's profits without showing any direct financial harm.[r7]  
* **The Doctrine of "Waiver of Tort":** Originating in seventeenth-century common-law courts of *indebitatus assumpsit* (rather than Chancery), this doctrine permits a plaintiff to "waive" their claim for compensatory tort damages and elect instead to sue in *assumpsit* for a disgorgement of the defendant's profits.[r63, r78] Because waiver of tort is a common-law doctrine, equitable defenses such as laches or the unclean hands maxim do not apply, and the disgorgement is a matter of right rather than judicial discretion.[r78]  
* **Section 39 and the Opportunistic Breach Controversy:** Section 39 of the R3RUE proposed a major expansion, authorizing disgorgement of profits as an alternative remedy for "opportunistic breach" of contract, where a party deliberately breaches to secure a more profitable transaction.[r91] This has drawn fierce academic criticism from relational contract theorists, who argue that Section 39 would undermine the cooperative, risk-sharing nature of commercial contracts and punish efficient breaches.[r91]

## **VI. The Contract-Tort Boundary and Liability Allocation**

### **Enforceability and Liability in Preliminary Agreements**

A primary source of litigation in international commercial transactions relates to the enforceability of preliminary agreements, such as letters of intent (LOIs), memoranda of understanding (MOUs), and term sheets.[r75] While commercial parties frequently utilize these instruments to outline deal terms prior to definitive drafting, courts analyze their enforceability through a three-part comparative taxonomy [r75]:

First, agreements are deemed entirely unenforceable due to the complete lack of definite terms or an objective manifestation of intent to be bound, representing mere "agreements to agree".[r75]

Second, highly detailed preliminary agreements may be recognized as fully enforceable, binding contracts if they satisfy all standard formation requirements (offer, acceptance, and consideration), notwithstanding that a more formal document was contemplated.[r75]

Third, there exists a broad middle area where preliminary agreements are unenforceable as a whole, but successfully serve as the basis for legal liability regarding independent obligations.[r75] These independent, binding obligations typically include: (1) an implied-in-law or contractually explicit duty to negotiate in good faith, (2) strict confidentiality covenants, and (3) exclusivity provisions barring parallel negotiations.[r75]

In this middle area, civil-law and common-law systems diverge.[r75] Civil-law jurisdictions aggressively enforce a pre-contractual duty of good faith (*culpa in contrahendo*), subjecting a party who abruptly terminates negotiations without a reasonable basis to delictual or pre-contractual liability.[r75] Conversely, English and American common-law courts are highly reluctant to impose a generalized duty to negotiate in good faith unless the parties have explicitly contractually agreed to such a standard.[r75]

### **Commercial Insurance: Duty to Defend vs. Duty to Indemnify**

Commercial General Liability (CGL) insurance serves as the primary risk-allocation mechanism for business liability, protecting firms against third-party claims of bodily injury, property damage, and advertising injury.[r92] The operationalization of a CGL policy is governed by a strict division of duties [r83, r108, r110]:

The duty to defend is the most valuable asset in a CGL policy, requiring the insurer to pay for the insured's legal counsel.[r92] It is exceptionally broad and is determined under the "potentially covered" standard.[r108, r110] Under the "Four Corners" or "Eight Corners" rule (prominent in Texas and other jurisdictions), courts compare only the allegations within the third-party complaint against the text of the insurance policy.[r83, r92] If the complaint contains even a single allegation that potentially falls within the scope of coverage—even if groundless, false, or fraudulent—the insurer must defend the entire lawsuit.[r83, r93, r110]

Conversely, the duty to indemnify is much narrower, arising only after liability is established, and is determined strictly by the actual, proven facts of the case.[r83, r93] The insurer’s defense obligation begins the moment the insured tenders notice of the complaint, and it persists until the insurer can prove with clinical certainty that none of the remaining claims are covered, or until the policy limits are exhausted by judgments or settlements.[r92, r108]

An insurer faced with a complex claim will often issue a Reservation of Rights (ROR) letter, agreeing to provide a defense while reserving the right to later deny indemnification or seek reimbursement of defense costs if it is determined that no coverage exists.[r83, r92] Under Texas and other state laws, defending under an ROR often creates a severe conflict of interest for the insurer-appointed defense counsel, who may have an incentive to develop facts that favor the insurer's coverage defenses.[r83, r110] Consequently, the insured gains a legal right to select independent, unconflicted counsel (commonly known as *Cumis* counsel) whose reasonable fees must be paid entirely by the insurer.[r83, r108]

Insurers owe a duty of good faith and fair dealing to their policyholders.[r83] An insurer that denies a claim without a reasonable basis, delays payments without justification, or fails to settle within policy limits when liability is clear may be held liable for bad-faith damages.[r83, r92] Bad-faith claims can result in damages that exceed the policy limits, including consequential damages, attorney's fees, and punitive damages, significantly increasing the insurer's financial exposure.[r83, r92]

### **Good Faith and the Abuse of Rights in Comparative Private Law**

The comparative study of private law reveals fundamental divergences in how common-law and civil-law jurisdictions handle contractual performance and the exercise of legal rights [r38, r85, r87]:

* **Treu und Glauben (Good Faith) in the German BGB:** In German civil law, the principle of good faith (codified in BGB Section 242) is considered the "king" of the Civil Code, serving as a powerful tool to moralize the entire legal order.[r12, r17] Under BGB Section 242, the debtor is bound to perform their obligation in accordance with the requirements of objective good faith, taking ordinary usage into consideration.[r12, r96] German courts have developed an elaborate classification of applications (*Fallgruppen*), utilizing Section 242 to: (1) particularize incomplete contracts by imposing secondary duties of disclosure and cooperation; (2) limit the abusive exercise of legal rights; and (3) adapt or cancel contracts when the underlying economic foundation of the transaction has collapsed (*Wegfall der Geschäftsgrundlage*).[r12, r17, r65]  
* **The "Abuse of Rights" (Abus de Droit) Divergence:** Under the civilian *abus de droit* doctrine, an otherwise lawful right (such as a property right or contractual power) cannot be exercised maliciously, disproportionately, or for an anti-social purpose contrary to good faith.[r72, r81, r106] This doctrine, pioneered by French courts under Article 1382 and codified in German and Colombian law, prevents landowners from building chimneys solely to block a neighbor's light, or creditors from executing sudden foreclosures to destroy a debtor.[r34, r72, r81]  
* **The Strict English Common-Law Position:** Historically, the English common law fiercely rejected the abuse of rights doctrine.[r34, r81] In landmark cases such as *Mayor of Bradford v. Pickles* (1895) and *Allen v. Flood* (1898), the House of Lords held that any right given by property ownership or contract may be exercised against another regardless of the motive, declaring that a malicious or "churlish" motive cannot make a lawful act illegal.[r34] This commitment to "unrestricted egoism" remains a hallmark of common-law contract literalism, although modern American law uses the doctrine of unconscionability to police extreme overreaching.[r34, r38]

## **VII. Structural Conclusions and Strategic Private-Law Guidance**

The horizontal private-law responsibility system coordinates human, entity, and asset relations through a self-executing framework of civil obligations, balancing party autonomy with mandatory public-law limits.[1, r10, s21] To manage private-law risk under conditions of legal and technological uncertainty, the strategic advisor must evaluate all civil liabilities through an integrated responsibility equation:

Liability = f(Duty, Breach, Causation, Admissible Proof) * Remedial Vector

where the Remedial Vector represents the chosen horizontal enforcement path (Compensatory, Restitutionary, or Gains-Based Disgorgement).[1, r2, r5, r45]

By synthesizing the ongoing restatements, state civil justice reforms, and technology-induced shifts across 2025 and 2026, three primary conclusions emerge:

First, **the expansion of product liability concepts into the digital world** represents a structural realignment of tort law.[53, s13] As courts reject the traditional tangibility-based "product vs. service" dichotomy to hold conversational AI developers and social media companies liable for defective, engagement-maximizing algorithms, private-law exposure has migrated upstream.18 Transactional engineers and compliance officers must establish rigorous "safety-by-design" audit trails and clear user warnings to mitigate design-defect and post-sale duty-to-warn risks, especially when dealing with vulnerable minor populations.[53, s13, s28]

Second, **subsurface pore-space sequestration and geologic carbon storage** require a transition away from the rigid common-law trespass model toward Schremmer’s modified, harm-based liability models.[r22, r28] To prevent commercial paralysis, property law must balance traditional rights of exclusion against the collective necessity of climate mitigation, utilizing statutory pore-space allocations to overcome common-law gridlock.[r22, r28, r39]

Third, **the contractarian triumph in Delaware LLC agreements** (*Khan v. Warburg Pincus*) highlights the absolute primacy of negotiated text in commercial relationships, warning fiduciaries and investors that the implied covenant of good faith cannot be deployed to revive waived fiduciary duties.[r26, r51] Conversely, in the trust and ERISA domains, the proliferation of AI-based analytics and the objective "should have known" standard have significantly elevated the fiduciary duty of prudence, charging trustees with a proactive, continuous duty to monitor and utilize modern diagnostic tools.[r25, r50]
